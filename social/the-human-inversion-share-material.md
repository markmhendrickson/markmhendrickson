### 1. Long-form X post — specialist identity crisis (post first — today or tomorrow, 4-6 PM CEST / 10 AM-12 PM US Eastern)

```text
Last week I posted the overview of a 5-part series on what happens when AI absorbs the execution middle of software teams.

Part I is about the inversion itself and what it does to the people inside it.

The architecture is net positive. Foundation and review were always where leverage differentiated — we just staffed execution because execution was expensive, and everything else got whatever was left over. Market research in the margins. Design systems built reactively. Coding standards in wikis nobody read.

When the middle collapses, those starved ends finally get the attention they deserved. That part is real.

The part nobody is being honest about: the people whose reputation was built on execution craft are watching the credentialing skill shrink. Judgment, taste, system intuition — none of that is automated. But the market has no vocabulary for valuing it independently of the execution it used to be bundled with.

Mid-career engineers on AI-heavy teams are asking whether they should become product managers. Not from passion. Because PM is defined by judgment and communication, and those are the skills that didn't atrophy.

The direction is often correct. The shape it takes — "my job is irrelevant, so can I become a PM?" — is a rational response to a transition nobody gave them language for.

The converse confusion is just as common: gravitating toward the skill AI just made accessible rather than the one it made scarce.

And there is a structural problem beyond identity. The human middle wasn't just doing execution. It was the implicit translation layer between disciplines. The PM's spec forced alignment with the designer because the designer had to read it. The design forced alignment with the engineer because the engineer had to build from it.

Remove the middle, and the PM's positioning doc is illegible to the engineer. The designer's constraint system is illegible to the PM. The architectural standards are illegible to the designer. AI produces the artifact. It does not resolve the cross-disciplinary tension that humans in the middle were resolving through handoffs and meetings.

Teams that don't restructure around this will look dramatically worse within eighteen months. Most won't — not because the technical shift is hard, but because the cultural shift requires things organizations find genuinely uncomfortable.

What does the restructuring actually look like from where you sit?
```

### Draft G — LinkedIn insight | Type: insight_post | Slot: Wed 7:30-8:30 AM US Eastern / ~1:30-2:30 PM CEST

```text
Software teams organized around execution for decades. PMs wrote specs, designers built mockups, engineers wrote code. Foundation work — market research, design systems, architectural standards — got whatever time survived after execution consumed the calendar. Review got worse treatment.

That wasn't a failure of leadership. It was an equilibrium imposed by economics. When execution was expensive, you had to staff execution.

AI changed the economics. The actual production of artifacts is where models are genuinely capable now. The execution cost didn't go to zero, but it collapsed enough that the center of gravity for human work has moved.

Humans now concentrate at the ends. Product people can spend time on real positioning and validated personas instead of writing specs for translation chains. Designers can invest in constraint systems instead of pushing pixels for handoff. Engineers can build architectural standards that structurally exclude classes of bugs instead of converting tickets into PRs.

The upside is real and substantial. But the experience of moving through it is not uniformly positive.

The people whose reputation was built on execution craft — writing good code, producing clean designs, drafting tight specs — are watching the credentialing skill shrink. Judgment, taste, and system intuition remain valuable. But the market has no vocabulary for pricing them independently of the execution they used to be bundled with.

Mid-career engineers on AI-heavy teams are increasingly asking whether they should become product managers. Not from passion, but because PM is defined by judgment and communication — the skills that didn't atrophy. The direction is often correct. The framing — "my job is irrelevant, so can I turn into a PM?" — is a rational response to an illegible transition.

There is also a structural problem beyond individual identity. The human middle wasn't only doing execution. It was the implicit translation layer between disciplines. The spec forced alignment between PM and designer. The design review forced alignment between designer and engineer. Remove the middle, and each discipline's foundational artifacts never had to be cross-readable because someone was translating in real time.

That translation has to come from somewhere now. The teams that figure it out first will compound advantages that are hard to close, because the compounding happens at foundation and review — the layers that were always supposed to matter most.

Part I of a series on what that restructuring looks like: https://markmhendrickson.com/posts/the-human-inversion
```
