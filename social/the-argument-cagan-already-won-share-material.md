## LinkedIn post | Type: insight_post

```text
When building got easy, the PM job got harder to explain.

Marty Cagan published what I think is the strongest defense of the PM role in December 2023. He named four critical knowledge domains: deep knowledge of customers, fluency with data and analytics, a thorough understanding of the business, and command of the competitive landscape. His conclusion was unambiguous: these contributions are "absolutely non-negotiable and essential."

By mid-2025, his rhetoric had shifted. The PM was now one possible "product creator." Anyone with product sense and AI prototyping tools could fill the role. Two articles published the same month said the opposite. One declared the PM role "more essential," while the other said anyone with product sense could do it.

The missed move is that if everyone can prototype, prototyping is no longer the PM's differentiator. The PM's value has to come from the integrative judgment that no amount of prototyping skill can substitute for: customer understanding, data fluency, business knowledge, and industry command, held in one head and applied as cross-dimensional judgment.

When delivery was expensive, integration failures were masked. Slow process was itself a forcing function. Every release got scrutinized through multiple lenses before launch.

Now that delivery is cheap, teams ship before anyone thinks through second-order effects. The PM-as-integrator becomes the critical check on a system optimized for speed but not coherence.

This isn't just about PMs. Every discipline is working through the same question: when the execution layer of your job gets absorbed, does your identity migrate to the thing AI just made easy, or to the thing it can't do?

Full piece: https://markmhendrickson.com/posts/the-argument-cagan-already-won
```
