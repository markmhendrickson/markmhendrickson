Last fall I spent a week in Silicon Valley catching up with friends across the tech industry. I expected the AI skepticism from people outside tech. I didn't expect it from engineers, product managers, and founders who work with the technology daily.

The pattern I noticed: skeptics don't just see AI's limitations. They conclude those limitations are reason to disengage. They approach it tentatively. They assume it won't improve fast enough to matter.

The enthusiasts see the same problems. But they encounter them more directly, because they work with the tech every day. They build new tools. They push through the friction. They treat the problems as the work itself, not as disqualifiers.

Here's the part I find striking: it's a litmus test about faith in human capability. The people who lean into AI believe humans can steer it. The people who pull back reveal doubt that we, as individuals or institutions, can guide the technology to a place that serves us.

There is no determined future. Nobody has written the script. A teacher rethinking what students need to learn is shaping AI's role. A small business owner automating invoices to spend more time with customers is shaping it. The question isn't technical skill. It's whether you engage with a productive mindset, willing to push through friction because you believe humans can make something good out of it.
