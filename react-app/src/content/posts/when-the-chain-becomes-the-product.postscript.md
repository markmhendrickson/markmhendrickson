*Added March 14, 2026:* Conversations on [X](https://x.com/markymark/status/2032711721689739587) and [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47295647) after publication helped sharpen a distinction this post implies but does not make explicit. The critical variable is not liquid ownership per se.

Liquid ownership always distorts product decision-making to some degree. Public companies go private for strategic turnarounds precisely because the price signal can overwhelm long-term strategy, and [research finds](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/28/2/342/1601139) that publicly listed firms invest substantially less and are less responsive to opportunities than comparable private firms, especially where stock prices are sensitive to earnings news (Asker, Farre-Mensa & Ljungqvist, *Review of Financial Studies*, 2015).

But post-product-market-fit, real usage data provides a counterweight that keeps the feedback loop more intact. The most dangerous combination is liquid ownership *before* product-market fit, when there is little or no product signal at all. The price becomes the only high-frequency feedback, and narrative fills the rest. That is the specific condition token-funded ventures create, and it is why the pattern described above is so consistent across them.
