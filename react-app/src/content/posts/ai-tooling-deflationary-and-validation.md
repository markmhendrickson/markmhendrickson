*Idea note from a conversation (March 2026). Not yet a full essay.*

## Working thesis

"AI tooling is deflationary" is half true. **Thin wrappers** (prompt packs, UIs that only expose the latest model) do get competed away as models absorb capability. **Infrastructure** that owns abstractions models do not provide—durable state, versioning, provenance, cross-session integrity—does not deflate on the same curve. Total cost of using AI (tokens plus human direction, review, integration) also does not go to zero; it may rise as people attempt heavier workflows.

A parallel thread: **selection** (find a validated market via research pipelines) versus **conviction** (build from pain you understand). Different failure modes: spreadsheet-discovered ideas you stop caring about in month four, versus products too few people need. "Pre-validated" usually means *signal that a problem is loud*, not that someone will pay, that timing is right, or that you will sustain execution.

## Possible outline

1. What people mean when they say tooling around AI is deflationary (wrapper risk, model release churn).
2. The counter: marginal generation cost is not total cost; token and supervision budgets can grow.
3. Split the category: tooling that wraps the model vs. infrastructure the model cannot replace.
4. Plug-and-play reliability as scarcity when generation is cheap (IKEA vs. custom shop).
5. Short section on validated ideas: complaint mining filters opportunities; it does not replace your own validation loop or answer "will I still care?"
6. Optional tie-in: empirical pull vs. push from real evaluators and design partners, not abstract debate.
