- The left scapegoats upward (wealthy expats, foreign buyers); the right scapegoats downward (immigrants, asylum seekers). Both directions appear in both countries: Spain has Vox alongside anti-guiri activism; the US has Japanese/Chinese property panics alongside anti-immigration politics.
- The evidence does not support the blame. Housing and wage problems are mostly structural. In Spain, foreign workers contribute 10% of total Social Security revenue while drawing about 1% of its expenditure (driven by a younger age profile), and the broader tourism sector generates roughly 16% of GDP.
- "Guiri" lumps together tourists, digital nomads, and long-term residents, but their impacts differ. The populations doing the most measurable harm (speculative short-term rental operators) are not defined by nationality. The populations defined by nationality (foreign workers, long-term residents) are net fiscal contributors.
- Political power runs the opposite way from wealth. Locals have the vote and regulatory voice at the levels where housing policy is set; EU expats can vote in municipal elections but not regional or national ones; non-EU residents cannot vote at all. Exit costs are asymmetric, but that asymmetry doesn't justify origin-based restrictions.
- "Punching up" is not philosophically defensible. The same logic, "their success is our hardship, restrict them," underpinned antisemitism and the Rwandan genocide. Severity escalates once the principle is accepted, as the Trump administration's ratcheting trajectory across two non-consecutive terms illustrates.
- Market-driven cultural change can feel coercive to people with less purchasing power, but the answer is to address underlying market dynamics, not restrict who can show up.
- The test for regulation vs. scapegoating: does the policy target behavior or origin? Entry policy inherently uses origin; the ethical problem is applying origin-based restrictions to people already participating in the local economy.
- The fixes are deregulatory or at least group-neutral: remove zoning caps, permit bottlenecks, and licensing barriers. Even interventionist solutions (public housing, inclusionary zoning) beat scapegoating because they target the problem, not a group defined by origin.
