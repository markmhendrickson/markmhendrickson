## Part I of VI — Two stories about masters and slaves

There are two stories that modern intellectual life inherits about the relationship between masters and slaves, the powerful and the powerless. Both were told in nineteenth-century Germany. Both claim to describe something fundamental about how human consciousness and culture develop. And they arrive at conclusions so different that the tension between them runs beneath almost every political and moral argument we still have.

The first story is Hegel's. In the *Phenomenology of Spirit*, two self-consciousnesses meet and enter a struggle for recognition. One risks death and becomes the master. The other submits out of fear and becomes the slave. But then something unexpected happens: the relationship inverts. The master, who does nothing but consume what the slave produces, becomes dependent and stagnant. The slave, forced to work and to discipline himself in service, develops genuine self-consciousness and the capacity to transform the world. Subordination becomes the path to a higher form of freedom.

This is an astonishingly optimistic story. It says that history has a rational structure, that contradictions resolve into higher unities, that even oppression is ultimately productive because it drives the development of consciousness toward mutual recognition and freedom. The slave's position turns out to be the generative one. The whole thing moves forward and upward.

If this sounds like a template for progressive political thought, that's because it is. The Hegelian structure — oppression generates consciousness, consciousness drives liberation, liberation achieves recognition — underwrites enormous swaths of modern politics. When someone says a group is "on the right side of history," they're speaking Hegel's language. When identity movements frame justice as a demand for recognition, they're operating in Hegel's territory. When critical theorists analyze how the experience of marginalization produces a privileged epistemic standpoint, the ghost of the master-slave dialectic is doing the work.

Hegel's story came to political life primarily through Marx, who kept the dialectical structure — history moves through contradictions toward resolution — but relocated the driving force from Spirit to material and economic conditions. Much of what we call progressivism inherits its sense of historical directionality through Marx, and through Marx from Hegel, even when it has no direct engagement with either thinker.

· · ·

The second story is Nietzsche's. In *On the Genealogy of Morals*, he covers what looks like similar terrain but works completely differently. He distinguishes between master morality and slave morality as two fundamentally different ways of generating values. Master morality begins with self-affirmation — the noble, strong, active person says "I am good" and only secondarily designates what is unlike them as "bad," which carries a sense of pitiable or common rather than blameworthy. Slave morality begins with negation — the weak, suffering, resentful person says "they are evil" and only then derives "therefore I am good." The slave's good is reactive, born from *ressentiment* rather than abundance.

Nietzsche's crucial and controversial claim is that slave morality won. Through the vehicle of Judaism and Christianity, the priestly caste achieved a transvaluation of values in which weakness became virtue, suffering became meaningful, humility became noble, and power became suspect. This wasn't liberation in any Hegelian sense. It was an act of spiritual revenge by the weak against the strong, accomplished through moral and religious means rather than physical force. And it was devastatingly successful — to the point where its values feel like the moral common sense of Western civilization.

> Where Hegel sees the slave's experience as ultimately productive and the dialectic as moving toward reconciliation, Nietzsche sees the slave's triumph as a catastrophe for human culture.

For Hegel, the dynamic between master and slave is driven by a logical structure — consciousness necessarily moves through these stages toward greater freedom. For Nietzsche, it is driven by psychology and physiology — by the affects of *ressentiment*, bad conscience, and the will to power operating in its reactive mode. Hegel's account is about the development of Spirit. Nietzsche's is about the genealogy of moral feelings in actual human animals.

There is also a crucial difference in what they think recognition means. For Hegel, the desire for recognition is fundamental to self-consciousness and its satisfaction is the goal of historical development. For Nietzsche, the demand for recognition from others is itself a sign of weakness. The sovereign individual doesn't need external validation. The master in Nietzsche's sense is precisely the person who doesn't care whether the slave recognizes them. This is almost a direct repudiation of Hegel's framework.

· · ·

These two stories generate two very different orientations toward morality, politics, and history — orientations that remain live and unresolved today.

If you follow Hegel's story, you'll tend to believe that history has a direction, that moral progress is real, that oppression contains the seeds of its own overcoming, and that the expansion of mutual recognition is both the trajectory and the goal of human development. You'll tend to see the moral evolution from aristocratic honor cultures to universal human rights as genuine progress — the gradual realization of what was always latent in human self-consciousness.

If you follow Nietzsche's story, you'll tend to see that same evolution as a catastrophic loss — the triumph of a reactive, life-denying morality over a vital, life-affirming one. You'll suspect that the language of rights, equality, and compassion is a tool of power rather than a description of moral reality. You'll notice that the people who most loudly proclaim universal moral truths tend to benefit from the particular moral order those truths legitimate. And you'll wonder whether the apparent moral consensus of the modern world reflects genuine insight or just the thoroughness of the slave revolt's victory.

Most people alive today operate within some version of the Hegelian story without knowing it. The idea that history progresses, that equality is a self-evident good, that compassion for the suffering is the mark of moral seriousness — these are so deeply embedded in Western culture that they feel like facts about reality rather than products of a specific moral tradition. Nietzsche's contribution was to show that they are products of a specific moral tradition, one with identifiable origins, identifiable beneficiaries, and identifiable costs.

This doesn't necessarily mean Nietzsche was right and Hegel was wrong. But it means the Hegelian story can no longer be accepted as the default. Once you've seen the genealogy behind the values, you can't unsee it. The question becomes: given that our moral framework is a historical product rather than an eternal truth, is it still the one we want? Is it still the one that serves life?

· · ·

One thing worth noting is that both thinkers share more than Nietzsche would have wanted to admit. Both are deeply historical, both see culture and values as products of development rather than fixed truths, and both are interested in how consciousness transforms itself. The disagreement is really about whether that transformation has a rational direction.

Hegel says yes — history is the progressive self-realization of Spirit through contradiction and reconciliation. Nietzsche says no — history is driven by will to power, accident, and struggle, and there is no guaranteed upward trajectory. Hegel's system, for all its talk of becoming and movement, ultimately arrives at rest — Absolute Knowledge, the end of history. Nietzsche's philosophy gives you eternal recurrence — the same tensions repeating forever, with no final synthesis.

There's an uncomfortable implication of Nietzsche's account that neither his admirers nor his detractors always face squarely. If slave morality is the moral framework of contemporary civilization — and he believed it is — then virtually all modern moral and political thought operates within it. The demand for equality, the valorization of suffering, the suspicion of power — these aren't specifically progressive or specifically conservative in Nietzsche's analysis. They're Christian in origin, and nearly everyone in the modern West is operating within that inheritance, whether they acknowledge it or not. The critique cuts much deeper than any contemporary political alignment, which is part of why Nietzsche remains so difficult to domesticate for any political program.

The tension between these two stories — one that sees history bending toward justice and another that sees that very narrative as a sophisticated instrument of power — is the tension at the heart of our current moment. We argue about it endlessly without naming it. Every debate about whether moral progress is real, whether identity politics captures something true about the world or distorts it, whether our institutions serve justice or merely claim to — these are arguments between Hegel and Nietzsche conducted by people who mostly haven't read either.

I spent my undergraduate honors thesis at Bowdoin trying to piece together Nietzsche's theory of morality across his major works, tracing how it developed from the early skepticism of *Human, All Too Human* through the devastating historical narrative of the *Genealogy*. Two decades later, I find that the questions I was grappling with at twenty-one haven't been resolved. They've become, if anything, more urgent — more visible in the daily arguments about power, values, and who gets to define what counts as good. The remaining essays in this series will try to follow those questions where they lead.

## Part II of VI — The moral inheritance nobody acknowledges

Ask a thoughtful progressive where their moral convictions come from and you'll usually get one of two answers. The first is that they're self-evident — that equality, compassion, and justice for the marginalized are simply what morality requires, and anyone who disagrees is either confused or corrupt. The second is more sophisticated: that centuries of moral progress have brought us to a clearer understanding of human dignity, and that the arc of history, while long, bends toward justice.

Both answers obscure more than they reveal. The convictions that feel self-evident have a genealogy. The arc that bends toward justice was built by specific thinkers who had specific — and often mutually incompatible — ideas about what justice means and why history should move toward it. Modern progressivism inherits from at least four major intellectual traditions simultaneously, rarely acknowledges any of them explicitly, and has never resolved the tensions between them.

· · ·

The most obviously inherited element is the sense that history has a direction. When people describe a policy as "backwards" or declare that someone is "on the wrong side of history," they're invoking a Hegelian framework in which moral and political development moves toward greater inclusion, recognition, and freedom. Hegel would recognize this structure even if he wouldn't endorse the specific content. His dialectic — thesis, antithesis, synthesis — described how consciousness, culture, and political institutions develop through contradiction toward richer, more comprehensive forms. The slave's position becomes productive. The contradiction resolves into something higher. History is rational.

This sense of historical directionality entered progressive thought primarily through the concept of recognition. Hegel's master-slave dialectic, as I discussed in the [first essay](#part-1), describes self-consciousness as developing through the struggle for mutual recognition. Alexandre Kojève's mid-twentieth-century lectures on Hegel transmitted this idea to a generation of French intellectuals. Charles Taylor's later work on the politics of recognition gave it direct political application. When contemporary progressivism frames justice in terms of visibility, acknowledgment, and the demand to be seen — when the central wrong is understood as the denial of recognition to marginalized groups — it's operating in broadly Hegelian territory.

But there's a tension here that progressivism rarely confronts. Hegel himself was in many respects a conservative thinker. He saw the Prussian state as something close to the rational culmination of political development. He believed freedom was realized through participation in established social structures — family, civil society, the state — not through liberation from them. His famous line that "the real is rational and the rational is real" was in part a defense of existing institutions against revolutionary idealism. A great deal of contemporary progressive thought would look to Hegel more like what he criticized in Kant and the French Revolution: abstract moralism that imposes an *ought* on what *is*, without taking seriously what existing institutions have already achieved.

There's also a question of whether modern progressivism actually does dialectics or merely does teleology. Genuine Hegelian thinking requires taking the opposing position seriously as containing partial truth, working through the contradiction, and arriving at something that preserves what was valid on both sides. That's a demanding intellectual discipline. Most contemporary political discourse — and this is true across the spectrum, not only on the progressive side — skips this entirely. It identifies the good position and the bad position and advocates for the good one. That's moralism, not dialectics. Hegel would find it shallow.

· · ·

The second major inheritance is Marxist, and it runs deeper than most progressives are comfortable acknowledging. This isn't a matter of explicit doctrine — very few progressives have read *Capital* or would describe themselves as Marxists. It's a matter of analytical habits that have become so pervasive they feel like common sense.

These habits include: the tendency to analyze social phenomena primarily through power relations between groups; the assumption that cultural ideas and institutions largely reflect and serve the interests of dominant groups; the suspicion that appeals to universality or neutrality mask particular interests; and the conviction that material conditions and economic structures are more fundamental than the ideas people use to justify them. You don't need to have read Marx to think this way. These assumptions have been absorbed into sociology, critical theory, and humanities education so thoroughly that they function as background assumptions rather than contested claims.

The most important transmission mechanism was the Frankfurt School and, more broadly, the tradition of Western Marxism. Gramsci's concept of cultural hegemony — the idea that ruling classes maintain power not just through force but by shaping the common sense of a society — has been enormously influential on how progressivism thinks about media, education, and culture. This is genuinely Marxist in origin, even when the people deploying the concept couldn't identify Gramsci.

But collapsing progressivism into Marxism is a serious error, for reasons that matter.

> Classical Marxism is fundamentally about class and economics. Contemporary progressivism has displaced class from its central position in favor of race, gender, and sexuality. This isn't a minor adjustment — it's a fundamental reorientation that many orthodox Marxists regard as a betrayal.

Adolph Reed, Vivek Chibber, and other left materialists have argued forcefully that identity-focused progressivism actually undermines class solidarity and serves liberal capitalism rather than challenging it. From their perspective, contemporary progressivism isn't Marxist enough. It has taken the analytical tools — power analysis, ideology critique, structural explanation — and detached them from the economic base that gave them their original force.

Meanwhile, progressivism is broadly comfortable with capitalism in a way that any serious Marxist would find contemptible. The mainstream progressive position is regulated capitalism with a strong welfare state. That's social democracy, which Marx explicitly criticized as insufficient. Most progressives want representation in existing institutions, not the overthrow of those institutions. A Marxist would say this is precisely the problem: progressivism recuperates dissent into the existing system rather than challenging the system itself.

· · ·

The third inheritance is Kantian, and it's the one that does the most actual moral work even though it receives the least acknowledgment.

When progressivism asserts that all human beings have inherent dignity, that rights are universal and inalienable, that people should be treated as ends rather than means, that the moral worth of an action depends on its principle rather than its consequences — all of this is Kant. The liberal rights tradition running from the Enlightenment through abolitionism and the suffrage movements to the civil rights era is fundamentally about individual dignity and universal moral law. It owes nothing to Marx and little to Hegel. It is its own tradition, with its own internal logic, and it provides the normative backbone that progressive politics actually relies on when it makes claims about justice, rights, and human worth.

The pragmatist tradition contributed as well — Dewey's influence on progressive education and democratic theory was enormous. Religious progressivism — the Social Gospel movement, liberation theology, the Black church tradition — has its own deep roots entirely independent of German philosophy. When someone advocates for expanding healthcare access or protecting voting rights, they may be drawing on any of these traditions, or on simple moral conviction, without any Marxist or Hegelian framework at all.

· · ·

The fourth inheritance is Foucauldian — and it is the one that makes the tensions between the other three most destructive.

Michel Foucault's major contributions are now so thoroughly absorbed into educated discourse that they feel like common sense. He demonstrated that power and knowledge are mutually constitutive — power produces knowledge, and knowledge operates as a form of power. He historicized categories that had been treated as natural — madness, criminality, sexuality — and showed them to be products of specific institutional and discursive arrangements. He argued that modern societies control people not through spectacular displays of force but through surveillance, normalization, and the disciplinary regulation of bodies and minds. And he insisted that even liberation narratives — the claim that we're becoming freer, more enlightened, more just — are themselves mechanisms of power rather than escapes from it.

What makes this inheritance particularly volatile is its origin. Foucault's analytical tools derive directly from Nietzsche — the thinker whose philosophy we'll examine in the [next essay](#part-3). The genealogical method, the insistence that moral and epistemic categories have ignoble origins in power relations, the suspicion that liberation narratives are themselves tools of domination — all of this comes from a philosopher whose conclusions are antithetical to progressive politics. Progressivism took Nietzsche's sword and used it to fight for the cause Nietzsche spent his career diagnosing as a symptom of civilizational sickness. There is a structural parallel here — between the critical intellectual who derives cultural power from championing the powerless and a figure Nietzsche describes in the *Genealogy of Morals* — that will become clearer after we examine Nietzsche's own categories.

These Nietzsche-derived ideas entered progressive politics through the academy, and their influence has been enormous. When contemporary discourse insists that categories of identity are socially constructed, that institutions produce the subjects they claim merely to manage, that knowledge is always implicated in power — this is Foucault's analytical framework, deployed by people who have often never read him or Nietzsche. His tools provide what feels like a sophisticated operating system for political analysis: the capacity to unmask hidden structures of domination, to show that someone's common sense is someone else's ideology, that someone's normalcy is someone else's cage.

But this framework is in direct conflict with the Kantian inheritance that does progressivism's actual moral work. If all categories are socially constructed and all knowledge claims are exercises of power, then the categories progressivism itself relies on — oppression, dignity, justice — are also constructs produced by specific power arrangements, not descriptions of moral reality. If liberation is always a new form of power rather than its absence, then the progressive project cannot claim to be anything more than the ascension of one power configuration over another. Every tool in the critical arsenal can be turned against the hand that wields it — and in practice it is, constantly, as different factions apply the hermeneutics of suspicion to each other's claims, generating an endless recursion of critique in which every position can be unmasked as a disguised bid for power, including the position of the unmasker.

Foucault himself could never resolve this. His analytical framework denied the possibility of a neutral standpoint outside power. His political practice — prison reform activism, solidarity with dissidents, advocacy for marginalized communities — consistently assumed exactly such a standpoint. Late in his career he tried to escape the bind by turning to ancient Greek and Roman self-cultivation, an aesthetics of existence that offered a way to engage with power artfully rather than morally. But the escape was never completed, and the incoherence passed intact into the discourse that inherited his tools.

The result, in its contemporary form, looks a great deal like something Nietzsche warned about: a nihilism that doesn't know itself. The tools of critique have become so powerful that they dissolve every positive commitment, including the critic's own — but this dissolution presents itself not as nihilism but as moral conviction, not as the absence of values but as the most urgent defense of them. The intensity of the outrage masks the absence of the foundations.

· · ·

Here is the problem these four inheritances create: they are in genuine tension with each other, and progressivism has never acknowledged or resolved the tension.

The Hegelian element says history has a rational direction and contradictions resolve into higher syntheses. The Marxist element says the Hegelian story is ideological mystification — ideas don't drive history, material conditions do, and the appearance of rational progress masks the reality of class struggle. The Kantian element says moral truths are universal and accessible to reason, independent of historical circumstances. But if Kant is right that moral principles are a priori and universal, then neither Hegel's historicism nor Marx's materialism can be the whole story — because genuine moral truths don't depend on where you stand in history or in the class structure. And the Foucauldian element takes the Marxist suspicion of ideology and makes it total — where Marx at least leaves a privileged position for the proletariat, whose interests align with historical truth, Foucault allows no such refuge. Every position is situated. Every knowledge claim is a power move. This dissolves not just the opposition's claims but the critic's own.

The Marxist power analysis, taken seriously, should apply to progressive knowledge claims themselves. If all knowledge is shaped by the social position of the knower, and if appeals to universality mask particular interests, then progressive claims about justice are just as situated and just as interested as the claims they oppose. The analytical tool dissolves its own foundations. But progressivism almost never applies its own hermeneutics of suspicion to itself — it reserves the genealogical critique for its opponents while treating its own moral commitments as simply true.

· · ·

The people most likely to insist that progressivism is fundamentally Marxist are conservatives who want to discredit it by association and a small number of actual Marxists who want to claim credit. The people most likely to deny any connection are progressives who find the association politically inconvenient. The truth is messier than any of them prefer.

A balanced assessment looks something like this: contemporary progressivism has absorbed significant Marxist analytical habits, especially around power, ideology, and structural critique, primarily through academic channels. It has absorbed Foucauldian methods even more thoroughly, to the point where "power/knowledge" and "social construction" function as the default analytical vocabulary of educated progressive discourse. But it has also fundamentally transformed what it inherited — displacing class for identity, abandoning revolutionary politics for reformism, and blending Marxist- and Foucauldian-derived analysis with liberal, pragmatist, and religious traditions that are independent of and sometimes incompatible with both. Calling it "fundamentally Marxist" overstates the coherence. Calling it entirely independent of Marx and Foucault ignores real intellectual history.

What progressivism most clearly is — and this is what makes it unstable as an intellectual framework even when it's effective as a political movement — is a hybrid. It has Hegel's sense of direction without his institutional conservatism. It has Marx's suspicion of power without his economic focus or revolutionary ambition. It has Kant's moral universalism without his philosophical rigor or his awareness that universalism needs grounding. It has Foucault's critical arsenal without his recognition that the arsenal destroys everything it touches, including the hand that wields it. These elements are held together not by theoretical coherence but by shared political commitments and a shared emotional orientation — the conviction that the suffering of the marginalized matters and that existing arrangements are insufficiently just.

That emotional orientation is real, and I don't think it should be dismissed. But it's not a philosophy. And when it borrows philosophical tools — dialectics from Hegel, power analysis from Marx, rights language from Kant, genealogical critique from Foucault — without acknowledging that these tools make competing claims about the nature of morality, knowledge, and history, it builds on a foundation it can't inspect without risk of collapse.

> The popular version is a flattened teleology — history moves toward justice — stripped of the philosophical machinery that made Hegel's version rigorous and interesting.

This matters because the unacknowledged tensions create real vulnerabilities. When a progressive critic uses Foucauldian power analysis to expose how dominant groups shape cultural narratives, and then pivots to Kantian moral language to assert that all people have equal dignity that must be respected, they've performed a philosophical bait-and-switch. The power analysis undermines the universalism. The universalism can't survive the power analysis. Both are deployed as needed, and the contradiction is covered by the momentum of political conviction.

Nietzsche, who we'll turn to more fully in the [next essay](#part-3), saw all of this coming — not the specific political form, but the underlying dynamic. He understood that the moral framework of the modern West was a historical product rather than a timeless truth, that it served specific interests while claiming to serve everyone, and that its continued dominance depended on people not examining its foundations too closely. The reluctance to name and confront the tensions within progressive moral thought is itself evidence for his diagnosis: morality maintains its authority partly by discouraging the kind of scrutiny that would reveal its contingency.

Whether that scrutiny leads to Nietzsche's revaluation of all values, to a more honest and self-aware progressivism, or to something else entirely is an open question. But the scrutiny itself is overdue. You can't build a durable politics on a philosophy that can't survive its own analytical methods.

## Part III of VI — What Nietzsche actually argued

In 2007 I wrote an honors thesis at Bowdoin College on Nietzsche's theory of morality. The thesis ran to 152 pages. It traced his argument across three major works — *Human, All Too Human*, *Daybreak*, and *On the Genealogy of Morals* — and situated those arguments against the moral philosophies of Arthur Schopenhauer and Paul Rée, the two thinkers who most directly shaped Nietzsche's own development. I concluded, at twenty-one, that Nietzsche's historical account of morality was potentially valid but that his call to invert our values — to restore selfishness to a position of honor — was more than I could accept.

Nearly two decades later, I find that the questions I couldn't resolve at twenty-one haven't been resolved by anyone else either. The academic literature on Nietzsche remains overwhelmingly focused on domesticating him — on extracting something compatible with liberal moral sensibilities from a philosophy that is fundamentally hostile to them. Scholars try to preserve fairness and justice while discarding the ascetic ideal, or to claim that Nietzsche believed in inherent human selflessness, or to recast his immoralism as merely a call for better morality rather than the annihilation of morality as such. These readings consistently flinch at the moment of greatest discomfort, which is the moment Nietzsche himself considered most important.

What follows is my attempt to present what Nietzsche actually argued — not the caricature, not the domesticated version, but the sustained case as it develops across his career. The argument is more coherent, more carefully built, and more dangerous than it usually gets credit for.

· · ·

The foundation is laid in *Human, All Too Human*, published in 1878, and it begins with a single devastating claim: there is no such thing as a genuinely selfless action.

Schopenhauer, Nietzsche's great philosophical predecessor and early idol, had built his entire moral philosophy on the reality of compassion — the capacity of one person to identify with the suffering of another so completely that the boundary between self and other dissolves. This compassion, for Schopenhauer, was the basis of all morality. It was real, it was metaphysically grounded in the unity of the underlying will, and it was the only force powerful enough to counteract the relentless egoism that otherwise governed human behavior.

Nietzsche's break with Schopenhauer is total. He contends that what we call compassion, selflessness, and unegoistic behavior are all sublimations of egoism — the basic element "seems almost to have dispersed and reveals itself only under the most painstaking observation," but it is always there. Every apparent sacrifice, every act of charity, every expression of concern for others is, at bottom, in service of the actor's own interests. Sometimes the self-interest is obvious — the desire for praise, for social standing, for the pleasure of feeling virtuous. Sometimes it is deeply concealed, even from the actor himself. But it is never absent.

This is not a cynical throwaway. It is the philosophical bedrock on which everything else rests. If there are no genuinely unegoistic actions, then the entire moral framework that valorizes selflessness and condemns selfishness is built on a fiction. The distinction between good and evil, as commonly understood, dissolves — not because nothing matters, but because the categories are wrong. We have been sorting human behavior into moral boxes that don't correspond to anything real about human motivation.

Nietzsche doesn't stop at denying selflessness. He goes further and argues that the belief in selfless motivation is maintained by a specific mechanism: self-deception. In a remarkable passage, he describes how a person who consistently pretends to be friendly actually becomes friendly — not because the pretense transformed him but because the deception became so thorough that even the deceiver forgot it was a deception. The friendly feelings are real as experiences but false as indicators of motivation. We lie to others about our motives, and then we forget that we lied, and then we believe our own performance. This process, operating across individuals and centuries, is how an entire civilization comes to believe in the reality of selfless virtue.

> The history of the moral sensations, Nietzsche writes, is a history of an error — one in which society progressively makes men accountable for their effects, then their actions, then their motives, and finally their nature.

Connected to this is his denial of free will. Like Schopenhauer and Rée before him, Nietzsche holds that the world is completely determined by causal factors. In a striking analogy, he likens the progression of human events to a waterfall — the motions appear spontaneous and free, but everything is in fact mathematically calculable. If human beings have no freedom of the will, they cannot be genuinely accountable for their actions. Praise and blame become incoherent. The entire moral apparatus of guilt, responsibility, and desert collapses.

Nietzsche understands that this is, as he puts it, the "bitterest draught" the man of knowledge has to swallow. But he believes it's true, and he foresees a future in which mankind gradually absorbs this truth and attempts to "transform itself from a moral to a knowing mankind." This new mankind is one in which man may no longer praise or blame but must stand before human actions "as he stands before the plants" — with curiosity and understanding rather than moral judgment.

· · ·

*Daybreak*, published in 1881, develops these ideas and turns the critique specifically toward Christianity. If *Human, All Too Human* establishes that morality rests on philosophical errors, *Daybreak* investigates how a particular morality — the morality of pity, rooted in Christian teaching — has made those errors maximally harmful.

Nietzsche defines morality as obedience to custom — nothing more. Moral codes are not discoveries about the structure of reality but accumulated habits enforced by tradition, fear, and social pressure. The specific content of a moral code is, in the deepest sense, arbitrary. What makes behavior "moral" is that it conforms to the prevailing customs, not that it reflects any truth about human nature or the good life.

But while all moralities are arbitrary in their foundations, they are not all equally harmful. The morality of pity — the specific moral tradition that descends from Christianity and dominates modern Western civilization — is uniquely destructive because of two features. First, it stresses the goodness of selflessness to the point of demanding that individuals deny their own drives and interests. Second, it stresses the evilness of selfishness to the point of making natural human desires a source of permanent guilt.

Nietzsche considers what this does to the actual lived experience of human beings, and what he finds is systematic torment. Because people are, on his account, thoroughly egoistic creatures, they cannot possibly live up to the demands of a morality that requires selflessness. The inevitable result is guilt — constant, pervasive, corrosive guilt. Christianity intensifies this by threatening eternal punishment for moral failure, creating what Nietzsche calls a "vale of misery" in which the faithful are tortured by the impossibility of satisfying an impossible standard.

The cruelty of this arrangement is worth dwelling on. The morality of pity doesn't just fail to help people — it actively makes them worse off. It takes the passions that are most natural and necessary to human life, declares them evil, and then punishes people for having them. Sexual desire becomes sin. Ambition becomes pride. Self-preservation becomes selfishness. The result is a civilization at war with its own instincts, a culture that has been taught to experience its own vitality as shameful.

Nietzsche also argues, counterintuitively, that pity itself — the emotion the morality of pity valorizes above all others — is harmful rather than beneficial. Pity replicates the suffering of the pitied in the pitier, increasing the total amount of suffering in the world without necessarily doing anything to alleviate the original cause. A person paralyzed by pity is less capable of actually helping others than one who approaches suffering with detachment and competence. The doctor who can set a broken bone without wincing is more useful than the one who collapses in sympathetic agony.

· · ·

*On the Genealogy of Morals*, published in 1887, is Nietzsche's masterwork on morality and the book that gives his theory its full historical depth. Where the earlier works argue philosophically that morality rests on errors, the *Genealogy* provides the historical narrative of how one specific morality — the morality of pity — came to dominate Western civilization.

The story, as I outlined in the [first essay](#part-1) of this series, centers on a confrontation between two types: the knightly-aristocratic type and the priestly type. The nobles originally designated themselves as "good" — strong, beautiful, happy, favored by fortune — and labeled the common people "bad," meaning merely pitiable or plain. This was master morality: a mode of valuation that began with self-affirmation and generated its negative concept as an afterthought.

The priestly type, physically weak but spiritually cunning, developed a monstrous hatred — *ressentiment* — toward the nobles who dominated them. Unable to defeat the nobles physically, the priests achieved a spiritual revenge: they inverted the aristocratic value system. The strong became evil. The weak became good. Suffering became meaningful. Humility became noble. Power became suspect. This was the slave revolt in morality, and Nietzsche locates its decisive historical expression in the triumph of Judeo-Christian values over the values of the Roman Empire.

What makes this account so powerful, and so disturbing, is that it reframes the moral evolution most people regard as progress — from aristocratic brutality to universal compassion — as a catastrophe. Nietzsche is not arguing that the nobles were morally superior to the slaves in the slaves' own moral terms. He's arguing that the entire moral framework that would allow you to make such a judgment is itself the product of the slave revolt. You can't evaluate the slave revolt from within the moral system it created, because that system was designed to make itself look like an improvement.

Nietzsche compares the two moralities on their own terms. Noble morality begins with affirmation and generates negation secondarily. Slave morality begins with negation — the designation of the enemy as "evil" — and generates its positive concept reactively. Noble morality doesn't require the fiction of free will. Slave morality depends on it, because without the assumption that the strong could choose to be weak, you can't blame them for being strong. Noble morality produces magnanimity, openness, and the capacity for genuine love of enemies. Slave morality produces suspicion, deception, and resentment disguised as righteousness.

> The morality of pity is so thoroughly victorious that it no longer sees itself as a morality at all. It appears to its adherents as simply the way things are — which is the most complete form of victory any value system can achieve.

The second essay of the *Genealogy* traces a parallel development: the emergence of bad conscience through the internalization of instincts forced underground by civilization. As human beings were compelled to live in organized societies, the instincts that had previously discharged outward — aggression, cruelty, the desire for domination — were turned inward. Man became his own torturer. The instinct for cruelty, denied its natural outlet, created the bad conscience — a perpetual inner accusation that became the psychological foundation for guilt, sin, and the entire edifice of moral self-punishment.

This is not, for Nietzsche, simply a disaster. It is also the process by which humanity acquired depth and interiority. He calls the bad conscience an illness "as pregnancy is an illness" — a condition that is painful and dangerous but also generative. Without the internalization of instincts, human beings would never have developed the inner life that makes them interesting. The soul, in a sense, is a scar — the mark left by civilization's violence against human nature. Nietzsche's honesty about this is one of the most remarkable features of his philosophy. He refuses the comforting stories told by both defenders and critics of morality. Morality didn't make us better. But it did make us deeper.

· · ·

The culmination of all three works is Nietzsche's justification for preferring noble morality to the morality of pity. This justification is grounded not in an external moral standard — he has just spent three books demolishing the possibility of such a standard — but in a descriptive account of what life is.

The will to power is Nietzsche's answer to the question of what drives all living things. Every organism seeks to discharge its strength, to grow, to extend itself, to overcome resistance. This is not a moral principle imposed on life from the outside. It is what life does. Noble morality is preferable because it is aligned with this fundamental character — it affirms strength, creation, self-overcoming, the discharge of vital energy. Slave morality works against it, valorizing weakness, passivity, and the suppression of instinct. An organism governed by a morality that makes it feel guilty for being what it is — an organism at war with its own drives — is an organism that is sick. Not morally deficient. Sick, in the way that a body fighting against its own immune system is sick.

The case is internally consistent across the entire arc of his career. In *Human, All Too Human*, he establishes that all action is egoistic and the belief in selflessness is a sustained error. In *Daybreak*, he shows how Christianity intensified this error by making selfishness not merely inferior but evil, producing a civilization of perpetual guilt. In the *Genealogy*, he provides the historical mechanism — the slave revolt — by which this life-denying value system achieved dominance over one that was life-affirming. The evaluative criterion remains stable throughout: what enhances life and the discharge of power is preferable to what diminishes it.

This is a real argument. It is substantially more than personal preference. But it rests on an interpretive foundation — the identification of life with will to power — that Nietzsche's own epistemology prevents from achieving the status of certainty. He tells us that there are no facts, only interpretations. The will to power is his interpretation — the most comprehensive he can offer, the one that accounts for the most phenomena, the one he thinks honest observation confirms. But it remains an interpretation. The justification for preferring noble values is persuasive rather than demonstrative. It says: look at human behavior honestly, strip away the moral self-deceptions, and you'll see that this is how things work. If you look and agree, the preference follows. If you look and disagree, the evaluative structure loses its force.

· · ·

At twenty-one, I concluded my thesis by acknowledging that Nietzsche's historical account of morality was potentially valid but that I couldn't accept his revaluation. I wasn't persuaded that selfishness should be elevated over selflessness. I worried about the consequences of a world that followed his prescriptions. I noted that he himself provides no guarantees — that the transition to immoralism might, in his own words, "put everyone under the necessity of carrying a gun."

I still feel the force of those reservations. But I've also come to see that the evasion practiced by the academic commentators I criticized in the thesis — the attempt to extract Nietzsche's insights while discarding his conclusions — is not an honest engagement with his philosophy. You can't take the genealogical method and leave the revaluation behind. The method was designed to serve the revaluation. If morality is a contingent historical product maintained by self-deception, and if it makes human beings sick by turning their instincts against themselves, then the question of whether to abandon it is not a question you can politely decline to answer.

Nietzsche knew his philosophy was dangerous. He called himself dynamite. He recognized that the revaluation of all values would be experienced as a catastrophe. But he also believed, with a conviction that borders on the prophetic, that the crisis was already underway whether anyone acknowledged it or not — that the moral foundations of Western civilization were already crumbling, and that the only question was whether mankind would face the collapse with honesty and courage or with denial and resentment.

Nearly a century and a half later, that question remains open. The [next essay](#part-4) will test this argument against its most obvious challenge: what happens to Nietzsche's account when you leave Europe entirely — and whether the diversity of the world's moral systems undermines his philosophy or vindicates it.

## Part IV of VI — A thousand moralities

The most serious challenge to Nietzsche's account of morality is also the most obvious one, and it has nothing to do with the internal consistency of his arguments. It's geographic.

The *Genealogy of Morals* tells a very specific story about a very specific historical sequence: a knightly-aristocratic caste whose values were inverted by a priestly caste through the mechanism of *ressentiment*, with the collision between Roman imperial power and Jewish monotheistic religion as the decisive pivot. "Rome against Judea, Judea against Rome." The entire narrative depends on particular features of the ancient Mediterranean world. Nietzsche grounds it in etymological evidence drawn from European languages and textual evidence drawn from European history. It is, unmistakably, a story about how the West became what it is.

The question is whether this is a universal account of how moralities form or a provincial one that Nietzsche mistook for universal. And the answer matters enormously, because if the master-slave dynamic is the template for all moral development, then Nietzsche has told us something fundamental about human nature. But if it's the template for one civilization's moral development only, then his philosophy — for all its brilliance — is a regional study dressed in universal rhetoric.

· · ·

Start with the most obvious counterexample. Confucian moral thought emerged from conditions that bear almost no resemblance to Nietzsche's schema. There is no slave revolt. There is no priestly caste waging spiritual revenge against a warrior aristocracy. Confucianism was articulated by members of the educated ruling class — Confucius himself was a minor nobleman and government official. The moral system it produced emphasizes hierarchy, filial piety, ritual propriety, and the cultivation of virtue through disciplined practice. It valorizes benevolence, but not in a way that resembles Christian compassion born of *ressentiment*. Confucian benevolence flows downward from the cultivated superior, and it presupposes rather than undermines social hierarchy.

On Nietzsche's framework, a morality articulated by the ruling class that affirms hierarchy and demands self-cultivation should look like a master morality. In some respects it does — the Confucian gentleman is defined by positive qualities he cultivates in himself, not by reactive negation of an enemy. But Confucianism also stresses restraint, duty, subordination of personal desire to social obligation, and care for others — qualities Nietzsche associates with life denial and the suppression of vital instincts. It doesn't fit neatly into either category. The master-slave dichotomy, which works tolerably well for the Roman-Christian transition, simply doesn't map onto the Chinese case.

Hindu civilization presents a different kind of challenge. The caste system does involve a priestly class — the Brahmins — at the top of the social hierarchy, and a warrior class — the Kshatriyas — below them. This looks superficially like Nietzsche's schema with the priests already victorious. But the Brahminical value system doesn't look like slave morality. It doesn't valorize weakness or suffering. It doesn't universalize compassion as the supreme virtue. It explicitly affirms hierarchy, duty according to caste, and the idea that different types of people have fundamentally different moral obligations. The warrior's dharma includes fighting and killing. The priest's dharma includes ritual purity and learning. There is no transvaluation in which priestly values delegitimize warrior values — both are affirmed within a single cosmological framework.

Japanese bushido combines elements that Nietzsche distributes across his two morality types in ways that shouldn't be possible on his account. It valorizes martial courage, honor, loyalty, and the willingness to die — clearly noble qualities. But it also demands extreme self-discipline, subordination to one's lord, and ritual suicide as atonement for failure — something that looks very close to the asceticism Nietzsche associates with priestly life denial. The samurai is simultaneously the blond beast and the ascetic priest, which Nietzsche's dichotomy doesn't allow for.

Islam emerged from a tribal warrior culture, was founded by a man who was both prophet and military leader, and produced a moral system that affirms conquest, courage, justice, and submission to God simultaneously. Muhammad is neither a Roman aristocrat nor a Jewish priest in Nietzsche's sense. He is something the framework has no category for.

> If Christian morality were the natural expression of something fundamental about human moral reality, then every civilization that achieved complexity and duration would converge on something resembling it. They don't. Not even approximately.

Indigenous and oral-tradition moral systems pose perhaps the deepest challenge of all. Many don't have sharp caste divisions between priests and warriors. Many don't have a concept of individual moral guilt in the Christian sense. Many organize moral life around kinship obligations, reciprocity, and relationship to the land — categories that don't map onto any of Nietzsche's distinctions. The moral universe of Aboriginal Australian songline culture or Amazonian perspectivism operates on premises so different from the European tradition that applying the master-slave framework isn't just inaccurate. It's a category error.

· · ·

So is Nietzsche's philosophy fatally provincial? The answer, I think, is no — but only if you're willing to revise what the philosophy claims, separating the universal engine from the particular narrative.

The strongest version of Nietzsche's position is this: the will to power always drives the formation of morality, but it does so in radically different ways depending on historical circumstances. The will to power is formally universal — all living things seek to discharge strength, overcome resistance, extend themselves, impose form on chaos — but materially particular. It doesn't produce the same moral configurations everywhere because it always operates within specific conditions. The will to power flowing through a desert tribal culture with a charismatic prophetic leader produces Islam. Flowing through a settled agrarian civilization with a literate aristocratic class produces Confucianism. Flowing through the collision of Roman power and Jewish monotheism produces Christianity and the slave revolt. The driving force is the same. The outcomes are radically different because the channels are different.

This is actually more consistent with what Nietzsche says about the will to power in other contexts. When he discusses art, he doesn't claim that the will to power always produces the same kind of art. Greek tragedy, Renaissance painting, Beethoven — these are all expressions of the will to power but they look nothing alike because they emerge from different conditions. There is no reason morality should be different.

And this version handles the counterexamples well. Confucian ethics doesn't need to be master morality or slave morality — it's the will to power expressing itself through a social formation in which the ruling class maintained power through ritual competence and moral authority rather than through priestly revenge or physical domination. The power dynamic is real — Confucian ethics served to legitimate and perpetuate elite rule — but the mechanism is distinctive. Bushido doesn't need to be an incoherent hybrid of noble and priestly elements. The samurai's asceticism isn't life denial — it's a technology for producing more effective warriors, will to power in service of ascending life even when it looks superficially like the self-mortification Nietzsche associates with priestly decadence. The form resembles Christian asceticism. The function is entirely different.

This revised Nietzsche is more modest in his specific claims but potentially more powerful in his application. He gives up the grand narrative of Rome against Judea as the universal template and gains a genuinely flexible analytical method that can be applied to any moral system in any culture. He preserves the will to power as the universal driving force while acknowledging that the forms it takes are as varied as life itself.

· · ·

But here's the turn that matters most, and the one I didn't see when I was writing my thesis twenty years ago.

The sheer diversity of the world's moral systems doesn't just challenge Nietzsche's specific narrative. It devastates the claim that Christian morality — or its secular descendants in human rights discourse and progressive egalitarianism — represents something universal about human moral reality.

China built and maintained one of the most sophisticated civilizations in human history for thousands of years on moral foundations that look nothing like Christian morality. Confucian ethics doesn't valorize the weak over the strong, doesn't universalize compassion as the supreme virtue, doesn't generate guilt through the fiction of free will, doesn't posit the equality of all souls before God. And it worked. It organized social life, sustained political institutions, produced art and philosophy and science of extraordinary depth. The same is true of Hindu civilization, of the Islamic world during its golden age, of pre-contact civilizations in the Americas.

If the moral framework that dominates the modern West — compassion, equality, universal rights, the moral priority of the suffering — were the natural expression of something inherent in human moral perception, then civilizations that achieved comparable sophistication would converge on something resembling it. The convergences that actually exist across cultures are far more minimal than what Christianity claims as morally essential: prohibitions on intra-group killing, norms of reciprocity, some form of fairness. The elaborate superstructure of sin, guilt, redemption, universal equality before God, and the moral priority of the meek and suffering is specific to one tradition and its derivatives. It is not a human universal.

This is the simplest and most decisive argument against the permanent authority of our inherited moral framework, and it requires no philosophical sophistication at all. It is an empirical observation. The person who wants to defend the universal validity of Western moral assumptions — whether in their Christian or secular progressive form — bears a burden of proof they rarely acknowledge. They need to explain why their moral configuration should be treated as uniquely correct when other configurations, grounded in entirely different premises, sustained civilizations of comparable depth and duration.

> The default assumption should be the Nietzschean one: this morality, like every other, is a human creation that emerged under specific historical conditions and serves specific functions, and its claim to universality is itself one of the tools by which it maintains its authority.

And this connects back to the will to power in a way that strengthens the argument considerably. If diverse moral systems are all expressions of the will to power shaped by different historical conditions, then the Christian claim to unique moral truth is itself an expression of will to power — specifically, the will to power of a moral system that discovered that claiming universality is an extraordinarily effective strategy for self-perpetuation. Telling people that your moral code isn't one option among many but the truth about how all human beings should live is a power move of the highest order. Through missionary activity, colonialism, and the secular universalization of Christian moral premises through human rights discourse, one provincial moral tradition has extended its reach across almost the entire globe. Nietzsche saw this. He observed that the morality of pity had become so thoroughly victorious that it "no longer sees itself." The comparative evidence makes visible what that victory obscured.

· · ·

This reframing also transforms the question of what comes after. Nietzsche's call for a revaluation of all values can sound purely philosophical — an abstract demand to question one's deepest moral commitments. But set against the comparative evidence, it becomes something more concrete. It's an invitation to look at the full range of moral possibilities that human civilizations have actually explored and ask whether the one we inherited is really the one best suited to our circumstances.

And the comparative perspective reveals that there are real resources available for this reevaluation, not just Nietzsche's somewhat sketchy gestures toward the blond beast and the sovereign individual. Confucian thought offers a sophisticated account of moral cultivation through hierarchy, ritual, and self-discipline that doesn't depend on guilt or the valorization of weakness. Stoic and Epicurean philosophy offer practices of self-transformation that are post-metaphysical without being nihilistic. Hindu and Buddhist thought offer accounts of self-overcoming that parallel Nietzsche's in striking ways but have been developed with far more practical detail over far longer periods. The warrior codes of various traditions — bushido, the martial honor traditions of the ancient world — offer accounts of excellence, discipline, and self-mastery that provide more concrete content than Nietzsche's noble type ever received.

The irony is considerable — and personal. When I wrote my thesis at Bowdoin, I concluded that Nietzsche's revaluation failed partly because nobody had articulated the affirmative vision he gestured toward. I surveyed the philosophers in Nietzsche's own lineage — Heidegger, Bataille, Deleuze, Foucault — and found that each developed the critical dimension with great sophistication but left the affirmative vision underdetermined. The [final essay](#part-6) in this series will examine what each of them tried and why they fell short. But the deeper problem was that I, like them, was looking in the wrong place. The actual articulations of non-Christian, non-egalitarian, life-affirming moral visions already exist in abundance. They come from traditions that the European philosophical canon has largely ignored or treated as anthropological curiosities rather than as live options for moral life.

This doesn't mean any of these traditions can be imported wholesale. They emerged under their own historical conditions and carry their own limitations. But they demonstrate that the moral landscape is far richer than the choice between Christian morality and nihilism that Nietzsche sometimes presents as the only options. The revaluation he calls for doesn't require inventing something from nothing. It requires the honesty to recognize that what we have is one option among many, and the courage to ask whether it's still the best one.

Nietzsche spent enormous intellectual energy trying to demonstrate through philosophical argument and historical narrative that Christian morality is contingent rather than necessary. The existence of a thousand other moralities settles the question empirically — quietly, decisively, and in a way that no amount of philosophical counterargument can undo.

## Part V of VI — The consistency test

Everything in this series so far has operated within a broadly Nietzschean framework — one in which moral claims are historically contingent, genealogically suspect, and ultimately expressions of power dressed in the language of truth. The thinkers we've examined, from Hegel through Foucault, all work within the Continental tradition where these assumptions, in one form or another, are the air people breathe. Now I want to introduce a thinker who starts from premises that Nietzsche would have rejected entirely, and who arrives — by a completely different route — at a conclusion Nietzsche would have recognized immediately.

Michael Huemer is a professor of philosophy at the University of Colorado. He is an ethical intuitionist, which means he believes there are objective moral truths and that we have direct intellectual access to them through a faculty analogous to perception. When something strikes you as obviously wrong — torturing an innocent person, punishing someone for something they didn't do — that appearance constitutes genuine awareness of a moral fact, not merely a preference or a culturally conditioned response. His principle of "phenomenal conservatism" holds that it is reasonable to trust how things appear unless you have specific grounds for doubt.

This is about as far from Nietzsche as you can get. Where Nietzsche says moral convictions are products of historical power dynamics, Huemer says they are perceptions of objective reality. Where Nietzsche says the feeling that cruelty is wrong is a symptom of slave morality's triumph, Huemer says it is an encounter with a moral fact as real as the redness of a fire truck. Where Nietzsche wants to dissolve the authority of moral intuitions by revealing their genealogy, Huemer wants to vindicate them by showing that they are our most reliable access to moral truth.

And yet. Huemer's most important book — *The Problem of Political Authority* — makes an argument that converges with Nietzsche's critique of moral fictions in a way that neither thinker would have predicted.

· · ·

The argument is devastatingly simple. Take whatever moral standard you already hold — whatever you believe about right and wrong, justice and injustice, the permissible and the impermissible — and apply it consistently to people who wield state power.

If it's wrong for me to lock you in a cage for growing a plant in your backyard, it's wrong when the government does it. If it's wrong for me to take your money at gunpoint to fund projects I consider worthwhile, it's wrong when a legislature votes to do it. If it's wrong for me to kill people in a distant country because I believe their leader is dangerous, it's wrong when a president orders it. The actions are the same. The consequences for the people on the receiving end are the same. The only difference is that one actor wears a uniform and claims an authority the other doesn't.

Huemer's question is: where does that authority come from? What is it about the state that transforms actions that would be obviously criminal if performed by any private individual into legitimate exercises of governance?

He considers every major answer that political philosophy has offered. The social contract — but no one actually signed a contract, and you can't be bound by an agreement you never made. Implicit consent through residence — but staying in the place you were born is not meaningful consent to anything, any more than a woman who doesn't leave her abusive husband has consented to the abuse. Democratic authorization — but the fact that a majority voted for something doesn't make it morally permissible, any more than a group of friends voting to rob one member of the group makes the robbery acceptable. Consequentialist necessity — but the claim that we need a state to avoid chaos is an empirical argument about outcomes, not a moral justification for a special right to coerce.

None of these answers work. Each one, when pressed, either proves too much (justifying actions we'd consider monstrous in other contexts), proves too little (providing only a weak, defeasible case that collapses under pressure), or is simply factually false (no social contract was ever signed). Huemer concludes that political authority — the special moral status that supposedly entitles the state to do things that would be wrong for anyone else to do — is a moral illusion. Nobody has it. Nobody ever had it.

> The power of the argument doesn't depend on whether moral truths are objective features of reality or contingent constructions produced by historical power dynamics. It depends only on consistency.

This is where the convergence with Nietzsche becomes striking. Nietzsche's genealogical method exposes moral fictions by revealing their contingent origins — by showing that what presents itself as timeless and universal is actually the product of specific historical forces serving specific interests. Huemer's consistency test exposes moral fictions by a completely different method — by showing that the moral principles people already profess, when applied without special exemptions, dissolve the legitimacy of the institution that claims to embody them.

Two different routes to the same destination: the discovery that the moral language surrounding political power is a fiction that serves the powerful.

· · ·

Nietzsche would have found Huemer interesting but would have pushed the analysis further. For Nietzsche, the question is not just whether the state's claimed moral authority withstands scrutiny but why people believe in it so readily despite its incoherence.

Huemer has an answer to this too, and it's one Nietzsche would have appreciated. He devotes a chapter to the psychology of authority, drawing on the Milgram experiments, the Stanford prison experiment, Stockholm syndrome, and the cognitive biases that make people defer to perceived authority even when it acts in ways they would otherwise condemn. His conclusion is that belief in political authority is sustained not by rational evaluation but by non-rational psychological mechanisms — conformity, deference, status quo bias, and the deep human tendency to obey whoever appears to be in charge.

This parallels Nietzsche's account with remarkable precision. In the *Genealogy*, Nietzsche argues that the morality of pity maintains itself not through the persuasiveness of its arguments but through the accumulated weight of custom, the mechanism of forgetting, and the systematic self-deception by which human beings conceal their true motives from themselves. The morality is so victorious that it no longer appears as a morality at all — it appears as the way things are, as moral common sense, as what any reasonable person would believe. Huemer says exactly the same thing about political authority: it is so deeply embedded in our psychological and institutional landscape that questioning it feels not just wrong but literally crazy.

There is a further connection that neither thinker makes explicitly but that emerges clearly when you read them together. One of Nietzsche's central insights is that morality functions as a tool of power while presenting itself as universal and disinterested. The morality of pity claims to apply equally to everyone — selflessness is good and selfishness is bad for all human beings — but in practice it serves the interests of particular groups. The universality is the disguise. The power dynamic is the reality.

Huemer's consistency test strips away exactly this disguise, though from a different direction. When the state claims moral permissions that would be considered monstrous if claimed by any private individual, it's doing precisely what Nietzsche describes: wielding power while dressing it in the language of universal moral principle. The state says "we are enforcing justice" or "we are protecting the common good," but what is actually happening is that one group of people is coercing another group of people, and the moral language is functioning as legitimation rather than description.

· · ·

But the convergence reveals something beyond what either thinker provides alone. It shows that the critique of moral fictions in political life doesn't require commitment to any particular metaethical position.

You can be a Nietzschean who believes all moral claims are expressions of will to power, and the consistency test still works — it shows that the state's own moral framework, applied honestly, delegitimizes the state. You can be a Huemerian intuitionist who believes in objective moral truths, and the genealogical analysis still illuminates — it shows how specific historical forces produced the illusion of political authority and how non-rational psychological mechanisms sustain it. You can reject both Nietzsche's anti-realism and Huemer's intuitionism and occupy some other metaethical position entirely, and the basic observation still holds: the moral standards that political societies profess are not applied consistently to the people who run them.

This is what makes the convergence significant rather than merely interesting. It suggests that the gap between moral presentation and political reality is not an artifact of one philosopher's assumptions. It's a structural feature of how political power actually operates. The state needs a moral exemption to function. Citizens need to grant it to feel governed rather than merely coerced. The entire arrangement depends on nobody pressing the consistency question too hard.

Huemer presses it. Nietzsche presses it from the other side. And the edifice, examined from either direction, turns out to rest on fictions that serve the interests of those who maintain them.

> The state fails both the genealogical test and the consistency test. Nietzsche says the morality is a construct. Huemer says even if it isn't, the state violates it.

There is one more dimension to this that connects to the broader argument of this series. The political function of morality, as Nietzsche describes it, depends on the appearance of uniformity — the fiction that the same moral standards apply to everyone. This fiction serves two functions simultaneously. It allows the powerful to present their coercion as morally legitimate by claiming they're enforcing universal standards. And it allows the weak to constrain the powerful by holding them rhetorically accountable to those same standards. Both sides need the fiction. Both sides deploy it. Neither side can afford to examine it too closely.

Huemer's argument disrupts this arrangement for both sides. The powerful can no longer claim that their coercion is morally different from private coercion — the exemption dissolves under scrutiny. But the powerless also lose their most effective rhetorical tool, because the moral framework they invoke to demand accountability is the same one that legitimated the authority they're challenging. If you follow the consistency test all the way, you don't get a more accountable state. You get no state — or at least, no state that can claim a moral right to exist beyond the brute fact of its power.

From a Nietzschean perspective, this is clarifying rather than destructive. Nietzsche wanted people to see moral language for what it is: a tool deployed in power struggles, not a description of objective reality. Huemer, despite being a moral realist, inadvertently demonstrates exactly this by showing that moral language as actually deployed in political life is shot through with inconsistencies that everyone tacitly agrees to ignore because the inconsistencies are useful. The moral realist and the moral genealogist, working independently, arrive at the same practical insight: the moral framework surrounding political power is a collective fiction maintained by mutual, unspoken agreement.

· · ·

I should be honest about where this leaves me. I don't fully accept Nietzsche's metaethics — the claim that all moral convictions are expressions of will to power, that there is no moral reality to perceive. But I also can't accept Huemer's intuitionism without serious reservations — the cross-cultural diversity of moral systems, which we explored in the [last essay](#part-4), makes it very difficult to maintain that our particular moral intuitions are perceptions of objective truth rather than products of our particular moral tradition.

What I can accept, from both of them, is the practical insight that moral language in political life functions primarily as legitimation. The people who wield state power use moral language to justify their coercion. The people subject to that power use the same moral language to constrain and challenge it. Neither side is doing what it claims to be doing — neither is simply describing moral reality or faithfully applying universal principles. Both are engaged in a struggle for power that moral language mediates but doesn't transcend.

Recognizing this doesn't tell you what political arrangements are best. It doesn't tell you whether the state should exist or what form it should take. What it does is clear away a layer of mystification that prevents honest engagement with the actual question, which is not "what does morality require?" but "how should we organize our lives together, given that we're all pursuing power and that none of us has a privileged claim to moral authority?"

That question — stripped of its moral disguises — is the one the final essay will attempt to face.

## Part VI of VI — The experiment

Over the course of this series, I've traced a line of argument from the competing stories Hegel and Nietzsche told about masters and slaves, through the unacknowledged inheritances — and deep incoherence — of modern progressivism, through Nietzsche's genealogy of morality built across three books and a career, the cross-cultural evidence that devastates the claim to moral universality, and the surprising convergence between a Nietzschean genealogist and a moral realist libertarian on the fictions that sustain political power. What I haven't done — what I've been deliberately deferring — is face the question that all of this analysis points toward.

If our moral framework is a historical product rather than an eternal truth. If it emerged from specific power dynamics and serves specific interests while claiming to serve everyone. If it makes people sick by turning their instincts against themselves. If its claim to universality is contradicted by the sheer diversity of viable moral systems that other civilizations have produced. If the political authority that enforces it can't survive its own consistency test. If all of this is true, or even substantially true — then what?

Nietzsche himself was asked this question, in various forms, throughout his career. His answer was consistent and, I now think, more honest than I gave him credit for when I wrote my thesis at twenty-one. He refused to prescribe.

· · ·

The refusal runs through his work from beginning to end. In *Daybreak*, he explicitly denies "the right to tender prescriptions as to the path to happiness." He calls instead for "novel experiments in ways of life and modes of society" and for mankind to "construct anew the laws of life and action" on "foundation-stones of new ideals" — ideals that would be determined not by any philosopher's decree but by the experimental process itself. He envisions "little experimental states" in which communities try different arrangements and discover through lived experience what works.

In the *Genealogy*, the refusal takes a different form. He acknowledges that morality has historical value — it made humanity deep, interesting, capable of self-governance. The bad conscience is an illness "as pregnancy is an illness." The sovereign individual, the "ripest fruit" of the morality of custom, is not someone who escaped morality's influence but someone who passed through it and emerged transformed. The morality of pity is not to be simply reversed, as though returning to pre-moral barbarism were either possible or desirable. It is to be overcome — transcended in a way that preserves what was valuable while shedding what was life-denying.

Nietzsche also recognized, with a candor that most of his followers lack, that this process would be dangerous. The transition to immoralism might "put everyone under the necessity of carrying a gun." The drive to knowledge could "drive mankind to the point of dying with the light of an anticipatory wisdom in its eyes." He acknowledged that a world without moral certainties would be terrifying, and he did not pretend otherwise. What he insisted on was that the terror was preferable to the lie — that honest uncertainty was a higher condition than dishonest conviction, even if the uncertainty carried risks that conviction did not.

> Nietzsche called for a moral interregnum — a period in which the old values have been recognized as contingent but the new values have not yet been determined. We may already be living in it.

When I wrote my thesis, I took this refusal to prescribe as a weakness — a gap in the philosophy where the positive vision should have been. I catalogued the scholars who tried to fill the gap and found their efforts unconvincing. I concluded that Nietzsche's revaluation was more than I could accept, partly because he couldn't tell me what came after it.

I now think that the refusal is the philosophy's greatest strength, not its greatest weakness. And I think this for reasons that Nietzsche himself provided, though I couldn't see them clearly enough at twenty-one.

· · ·

The central reason is this: if the will to power always drives moral formation but produces radically different outcomes depending on historical circumstances — the argument I developed in the [fourth essay](#part-4) — then there is no single correct moral configuration for all peoples at all times. The demand for a universal answer to the question "how should we live?" is itself a residue of the universalizing tendency of slave morality, which insists that one set of values must apply everywhere and always. Nietzsche's refusal to provide such an answer is not an evasion. It is a consequence of taking his own philosophy seriously.

Different communities with different compositions, different histories, different challenges, and different levels of health would need different arrangements. The "novel experiments in ways of life" that Nietzsche calls for aren't experiments aimed at discovering the one true morality. They're experiments aimed at finding what works for particular people in particular circumstances, with the understanding that what works may change as circumstances change. The sovereign individual is not someone who has found the right values. It is someone who has the strength and self-awareness to create values appropriate to their situation and to revise them when the situation demands it.

This is a genuinely distinctive political vision. It is not anarchism — Nietzsche doesn't argue that political authority is inherently illegitimate. He isn't opposed to hierarchy or command as such. The nobleman is by nature a commander, and Nietzsche nowhere suggests that commanding is wrong. What he objects to is the specific moral framework that currently legitimates political authority — the framework of universal equality, rights, and the morality of pity — not the existence of authority itself.

Nor is it libertarianism, despite the surface resemblance. Huemer arrives at skepticism of political authority through the consistency test — the state can't justify its own coercive power by its own moral standards. Nietzsche arrives at something different: not that the state violates morality, but that the morality the state claims to serve is itself a historical artifact that no longer serves life. The state fails not because it is immoral but because the morality that legitimates it is exhausted.

And it is not a call to return to master morality, despite what careless readers have always assumed. The passage from the *Genealogy* that my thesis dwelled on is unambiguous: the sovereign individual emerges at the end of the morality of custom, not as its antithesis. He is the fruit of the entire painful history of moralization — not a reversion to pre-moral nobility but something new, something that could not have existed without the discipline, interiority, and self-consciousness that morality produced. Nietzsche's vision points forward, not backward. The trouble is that "forward" is necessarily indeterminate.

· · ·

The thinkers who came after Nietzsche and tried to fill in the positive content confirmed, each in their own way, the difficulty of the task.

Heidegger took Nietzsche's diagnosis of nihilism and tried to locate it within a still larger story about the forgetting of Being. His positive vision — a recovered openness, a dwelling that lets things be — has a hypnotic quality but resists translation into anything livable. And his catastrophic political judgment — the embrace of National Socialism — suggests that the positive vision was never substantial enough to provide real orientation. If the thinker who spent the most time articulating what comes after nihilism couldn't distinguish authentic dwelling from fascism, the affirmative content remained dangerously underdetermined.

Bataille embraced Nietzsche's vitalism more directly and tried to describe what expenditure, excess, and transgression look like as lived experience. His writing about eroticism, sacrifice, and sovereign moments captures something real about the Dionysian — but it has the quality of peak experience rather than sustained way of life. You can't live permanently in ecstatic transgression. The sovereign moment is exceptional by definition.

Deleuze may have been the most philosophically rigorous. His reading of Nietzsche reinterpreted the will to power as fundamentally creative — the power of difference, of becoming, of generating new forms of life. His concepts are generative, and they've influenced art, architecture, and political thought. But when you ask what a Deleuzian existence looks like in practice, the answers tend toward aesthetic avant-gardism or permanent revolutionary posture — neither of which constitutes a way of life that most people could recognize as their own.

Foucault, as I argued in the [second essay](#part-2), had the moral appeal without the consistency. His late turn to ancient self-cultivation pointed toward something genuinely promising — an aesthetics of existence that engaged with power artfully rather than morally — but the project was never completed. Hadot, studying ancient philosophy as a way of life, had the practical concreteness but lacked the Nietzschean ambition. Each developed one dimension of the problem at the expense of others.

But as I argued in the [fourth essay](#part-4), these thinkers were all looking for the answer within the European philosophical tradition that produced the question. The actual articulations of non-Christian, non-egalitarian, life-affirming moral visions already exist — in Confucian self-cultivation, in Stoic spiritual exercises, in Hindu and Buddhist accounts of self-overcoming, in the warrior codes of various traditions. These aren't philosophical speculations about what a post-moral life might look like. They're records of how billions of people actually lived for thousands of years under moral configurations radically different from our own.

The revaluation Nietzsche called for doesn't require inventing something from nothing. It requires expanding the horizon of what we consider possible.

· · ·

But the experimental impulse hasn't only found expression in ancient moral traditions or twentieth-century Continental philosophy. It has shown up — in a form Nietzsche might not have recognized but would have understood — in the technological and political imagination of the last few decades.

In 1997, James Dale Davidson and William Rees-Mogg published *The Sovereign Individual*, which argued that information technology would progressively dissolve the nation-state's monopoly on coercion by making it impossible to tax, surveil, and control individuals whose wealth and work existed in cyberspace rather than physical territory. The language is explicitly Nietzschean without citing Nietzsche: the sovereign individual, freed from collective obligation and inherited institutional authority, creates value and organizes life on terms chosen rather than imposed. The book treats the moral and political authority of the state not as legitimate governance but as a resource strategy that becomes unenforceable when the underlying technology shifts. Twenty-five years later, much of what they described — digital currency, borderless work, jurisdictional arbitrage, the erosion of institutional trust — has materialized, even if the timeline and mechanisms differed from their predictions.

Balaji Srinivasan's *The Network State* takes the next step: not just individual sovereignty within existing political structures but the creation of entirely new political entities — network-organized communities that begin online, develop shared values and governance, acquire territory, and eventually achieve diplomatic recognition. Where Davidson and Rees-Mogg described the dissolution of inherited authority, Srinivasan describes the construction of new authority from first principles. His "one commandment" — the moral proposition around which a network state coheres — is explicitly a value choice, not a truth claim. It is closer to Nietzsche's "novel experiments in ways of life" than almost anything in the academic philosophical tradition.

What these thinkers share with Nietzsche — and what the philosophers who inherited him mostly lacked — is concreteness. Nietzsche called for experimental states. Davidson and Rees-Mogg described the technological conditions that make them possible. Srinivasan is trying to build them. The line from "little experimental states" in *Daybreak* to network states organized around a chosen moral proposition is direct, even if Nietzsche would have had complicated feelings about the specific values these communities might choose.

And cryptocurrency — Bitcoin in particular — occupies a distinctive place in this lineage. Self-custodied digital money is, at its core, a technology for acting in the world without requesting permission from the institutions that currently administer economic life. It doesn't argue against the state's moral authority in the way Huemer does. It routes around it. The person who holds their own keys and transacts without intermediation has, in a narrow but real sense, already accomplished what Nietzsche's sovereign individual is supposed to represent: the capacity to act from self-authored principles rather than inherited institutional permissions. The moral furniture hasn't been questioned philosophically. It's been made structurally optional.

· · ·

There is a more personal reason I've come to respect Nietzsche's refusal to prescribe.

The thinkers who come closest to living the affirmative vision rather than theorizing it tend not to be philosophers at all. They're artists, athletes, founders, builders — people who create new forms and impose new values through action rather than argument. The sovereign individual doesn't write treatises about sovereignty. He lives it. The philosophical articulation may be inherently inadequate to the thing it describes.

I wrote my thesis on Nietzsche at twenty-one. Then I spent nearly two decades building things rather than writing about philosophy — and that experience changed how I read every argument in this series.

After college I joined TechCrunch, then co-founded Plancast, a social event platform, and ran it through acquisition. I spent years as a product builder for early-stage startups. Then I spent seven years working on the Bitcoin and Stacks ecosystem — first building developer tools for digital ownership at Hiro, then running Leather, a self-custody Bitcoin wallet. The thread connecting all of it, which I didn't fully see until I came back to these questions, was sovereignty: not as a philosophical abstraction but as a design problem. How do you build systems where the user holds the keys — to their data, their money, their identity — rather than renting access from institutions whose incentives diverge from theirs?

Now I'm building [Neotoma](/posts/truth-layer-agent-memory), a truth layer for AI agent memory — local-first, user-controlled, inspectable, with provenance and deterministic state rather than opaque platform memory. The premise is that as AI agents become autonomous economic actors, the question of who controls what they know and remember becomes a sovereignty question of the first order. If your agent's memory belongs to a platform, you've outsourced your epistemic foundation. If it belongs to you — [structured, auditable, replayable](/posts/building-structural-barriers) — you retain the capacity to understand and direct your own tools.

I'm doing this from Barcelona, working with [agents as a team](/posts/working-with-agents-as-a-team), running a monorepo that contains my strategy documents, execution scripts, website, and agent playbooks in one place. It is, quite literally, one of Nietzsche's "little experimental states" — a single-person operation organized around self-authored principles, accountable to no institutional authority, building infrastructure for others who want to operate similarly.

The will to power is not an abstraction when you've spent years trying to bring something new into existence against resistance. The difference between reactive and active modes of existence is not a theoretical distinction when you've lived both. The sovereign individual — someone with the strength to create values rather than inherit them, to take responsibility for their choices without hiding behind received moral frameworks — is not an idealized figure when you've met people who approximate it and felt the difference between them and people who don't.

This doesn't resolve the philosophical questions. It doesn't tell me whether Nietzsche's metaethics is correct or whether Huemer's intuitionism offers a better account of moral knowledge. It doesn't settle whether the will to power is the fundamental character of life or one interpretation among others. But it does suggest that the answers, if they come, will come through living rather than through argument — through the kind of experimental engagement with life that Nietzsche called for and that philosophy, by its nature, can only gesture toward.

> The question at the end of the revaluation is not "what should we believe?" It is "what are we strong enough to create?"

· · ·

Let me try to say plainly where this series has brought me.

I believe that the moral framework most of us inherited — the Western synthesis of Christian compassion, Enlightenment universalism, and progressive egalitarianism — is a historical product, not an eternal truth. I believe the genealogical evidence, the cross-cultural evidence, and the consistency test all point in the same direction: this framework is one option among many, it serves specific interests while claiming to serve everyone, and its authority depends on people not examining its foundations too closely.

I also believe that recognizing this does not require nihilism. The existence of a thousand viable moral traditions across human history proves that moral life is possible — indeed, rich and sophisticated moral life is possible — without the specific framework we happen to have inherited. The revaluation is not a descent into moral chaos. It's an expansion of moral possibility.

I believe Nietzsche was right that the revaluation will be dangerous. He was right that it will feel like a catastrophe to those who have invested everything in the existing framework. He was right that it will require courage — not the courage of conviction, which is cheap, but the courage to live without the certainties that conviction provides. And he was right that no philosopher can tell you in advance what the result will look like, because the result is not a theory but a way of life that has to be discovered through practice.

I believe he was also right about something he said in *Ecce Homo*, the passage that opens my thesis: that his philosophy would be associated with "a crisis without equal on earth, the most profound collision of conscience." But I've come to read the passage differently than I did at twenty-one. The crisis is not something that arrives catastrophically from outside. It's something that develops slowly, as more people notice the gap between what morality claims and what morality does, between the values we profess and the lives we actually lead. The collision of conscience is not a single event but a long, uneven process of honest self-examination — exactly the process Nietzsche spent his career trying to provoke.

Whether that process leads to something better or something worse is not guaranteed. Nietzsche didn't guarantee it. He offered no guarantees at all. What he offered was the invitation — addressed to anyone strong enough to accept it — to stop pretending that the moral furniture of the modern world is nailed to the floor and to begin the difficult, exhilarating, genuinely dangerous work of asking what we would build if we knew we were free to build anything.

That invitation remains open. These essays are my attempt to take it seriously.
