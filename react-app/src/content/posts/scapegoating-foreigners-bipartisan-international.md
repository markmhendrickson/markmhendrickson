There's a protest meme that's been circulating in Barcelona for a few years now: *Tourists go home.* I've seen it on my own street, sprayed on walls in the Gòtic, chanted at demonstrations in the Barceloneta, printed on stickers slapped across Airbnb lockboxes. In the summer of 2024, [the meme went physical](https://www.cnn.com/): thousands of protesters marched through the Barceloneta spraying tourists with water guns, driving bewildered diners from restaurant patios mid-meal.[^1] The images went global. [Repeat demonstrations followed in 2025](https://www.npr.org/), coordinated across Barcelona, Mallorca, and other Spanish cities.[^2]

The water gun protest was widely covered as quirky, even charming. But deliberately spraying strangers is, under Spanish law, a form of *maltrato de obra* (minor physical mistreatment).[^3] That we instinctively read it as harmless says something about which groups we consider acceptable targets.

The sentiment is familiar even if you've never set foot in Catalonia. Swap "tourists" for "immigrants," swap Barcelona for any mid-sized American city, and you'd have a MAGA rally.

That comparison makes people uncomfortable on both sides. The Barcelona left doesn't want to be compared to American nativism. The American right doesn't want to be lumped in with European progressives. The magnitude differs, the policy tools differ, but the structural logic is identical. The main thing that actually varies is the socioeconomic direction of the scapegoating, and that distinction doesn't survive scrutiny.

## The same playbook

Both movements channel genuine material grievances (housing affordability in Barcelona, wage stagnation in the US) and direct them at a visible "other" defined by national origin. In Barcelona, that's the [*guiri*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guiri): the foreign tourist, the digital nomad, the Northern European expat buying a flat in Gràcia. In the US, it's the immigrant: undocumented workers, asylum seekers, anyone who arrived from somewhere poorer.

The emotional logic is the same in both cases. *Our place is being taken from us by people who don't belong here.* And in both cases, the proposed solution follows the same template: use collective political power to restrict the out-group's access to housing, work, or public space.

Barcelona's policy toolkit includes banning all short-term tourist rentals by 2028,[^4] proposing restrictions on non-EU foreign property purchases,[^5] and implementing strict rent controls that disproportionately affect foreign landlords.[^20] America's toolkit includes border walls, deportation campaigns, and work authorization restrictions. Different targets, same mechanism: identify a class of people by where they were born, then curtail their economic participation.

The comparison doesn't only run between Spain's left and America's right. Both countries have both versions.

Spain has Vox, its own right-wing anti-immigration movement, which channels resentment toward lower-income immigrants from North Africa and Latin America using rhetoric interchangeable with an American nativist playbook.

America has its own version of targeting wealthy foreign buyers: Florida passed a law in 2023 banning certain Chinese nationals from purchasing property in the state, drawing comparisons to the xenophobic [alien land laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alien_land_laws) of the early twentieth century.[^7] Critics within the Asian American community called it racist; supporters framed it as protecting local housing affordability and national security. Barcelona's proposed ban on non-EU foreign property purchases could have been drafted from the same template.

The political spectrum doesn't determine which outsiders get blamed. It determines which *direction* the blame flows. The left scapegoats upward; the right scapegoats downward. Both are scapegoating.

## The causal story is equally weak in both cases

If you're going to restrict a group's freedoms, you'd better have strong evidence that they're actually causing the problem you're trying to solve. In neither case does the evidence support the weight being placed on it.

In Barcelona, foreign buyers account for roughly 25–30% of property purchases[^8], a significant share. But the decade-long trend in total Spanish property transactions has been strongly upward, nearly doubling since 2013, driven primarily by domestic buyers who make up the vast majority of the market. The foreign share spiked to record levels in late 2023 not because of a surge in foreign buying but because ECB rate hikes temporarily crushed local mortgage-dependent demand: local buyers fell 25% year-over-year in Q4 2023 while foreign buyers (who more often purchase without Spanish mortgages) held steady.[^9] The headline "record foreign market share" was largely a denominator effect from a cyclical correction, not evidence of foreigners taking over the market.

Peer-reviewed econometric research on Airbnb's impact found that short-term rentals raised rents by about 1.9% citywide on average, and by up to 7% in the most tourist-heavy neighborhoods, with transaction prices rising 4.6% citywide and up to 17% in high-activity areas.[^10] Those neighborhood-level effects are real and painful. But they aren't doubling or tripling prices as protest rhetoric implies. And to the extent that short-term rental regulation targets the *behavior* of converting housing to tourist use, it's legitimate policy by any standard: a Catalan investor running the same Airbnb arbitrage does identical damage, and a ban that applies to all operators regardless of nationality is regulation, not scapegoating. Barcelona had no comprehensive tourist accommodation zoning until the [PEUAT](https://ajuntament.barcelona.cat/pla-allotjaments-turistics/en) was adopted in 2017, and the revised plan didn't take full effect until 2022; Airbnb's explosive growth happened in that regulatory vacuum, with thousands of unlicensed listings operating for years before enforcement caught up.[^24] The problem is when the policy response shifts from targeting the behavior to targeting the person's origin, as with proposed bans on non-EU property purchases. The STR damage was caused by a regulatory failure, not by who held the passport.

Meanwhile, the deeper structural drivers get buried beneath the simpler narrative of *the foreigners did it.* Spain's housing starts have fallen to levels not seen since the early 1970s.[^11] Roughly 75,000 Barcelona homes sit genuinely vacant.[^12] The 30% social housing quota on new developments has paradoxically deterred construction.[^13] The core problem is a structural gap between household formation and construction output, combined with a generational shift from ownership to renting and internal migration concentrating demand in the cities where supply is most constrained, dynamics overwhelmingly driven by Spanish nationals, not foreigners.[^14][^15][^16]

The American case is a mirror image. The economics literature on immigration and wages is genuinely contested, with estimates ranging from slightly positive to a 6-7% reduction for native low-skill workers.[^17][^18] But the most important drivers of wage stagnation, technological change, offshoring, the decline of unions, financialization, are structural, not demographic. Undocumented workers contribute roughly $90 billion annually in taxes and hold $299 billion in spending power.[^19]

A fair analysis requires distinguishing between the very different populations that *guiri* lumps together. A tourist on a three-day trip contributes spending and leaves. A digital nomad earning a foreign salary and renting an Airbnb competes directly with locals for housing without contributing to local labor markets or Social Security. A long-term expat on a Spanish employment contract pays into Social Security and, if they hold EU citizenship, can vote in municipal elections. The Social Security data applies to foreign workers, not tourists. The Airbnb research applies to short-term rental operators, not long-term residents. Pulling these apart actually strengthens the case against origin-based restrictions, because the populations doing the most measurable harm (speculative short-term rental operators) are not defined by nationality, while the populations defined by nationality (foreign workers, long-term residents) are net fiscal contributors.

In both cases, the scapegoated group is, on net, subsidizing the locals more than they're burdening them. In Spain the numbers are stark: international visitors spent over €134 billion in 2025; the broader tourism sector contributed roughly €260 billion, about 16% of GDP.[^21] Foreign workers contributed about 10% of total Social Security revenue while accounting for only about 1% of its expenditure, driven largely by the immigrant workforce's younger age profile.[^22] The fiscal transfer runs decisively from foreigners to locals, not the other way around.

And in both cases, group-neutral fixes exist: remove the zoning and permitting barriers that prevent housing from being built, open legal labor markets so workers aren't trapped in black-market exploitation, enforce contracts and property rights universally. None of these require identifying a villain by national origin. They require getting bad rules out of the way.

## The power asymmetry runs the other way

Here's where the conventional progressive framing breaks down completely.

The standard defense of anti-guiri politics goes something like this: *It's different because the foreigners in Spain are rich. They have more power. Restricting them isn't like restricting poor immigrants, it's punching up.* This reasoning looks at individual economic power and concludes that a wealthy expat facing regulatory friction is categorically different from a poor migrant facing deportation.

But this conflates individual economic power with collective political power, and when you look at the political dimension, the asymmetry reverses.

A Spanish citizen in Barcelona, even a struggling one, can vote in municipal, regional, and national elections. They can join a tenants' union with real political leverage. They can run for office. They participate in the regulatory process that determines whether an expat can rent, buy, work, or stay.

Most expats have little or none of that. EU citizens resident in Spain can vote in and stand for municipal elections, a meaningful form of participation. But non-EU residents cannot vote at any level, and no foreigner votes in regional or national elections, where housing and immigration policy are actually set. All expats are subject to rules they had no part in shaping at the levels that matter most, and when those rules change, as they do frequently in Barcelona's volatile housing policy environment, they have no recourse except compliance or departure.

This is structurally identical to the situation of immigrants in the US. American citizens, even economically marginal ones, have the franchise. They have representatives. They have collective bargaining power through political institutions. The undocumented immigrant has none of that.

I have skin in this game. I'm an American expat earning a Silicon Valley salary while paying Barcelona rents, which makes me exactly the kind of person this essay defends. That doesn't make the argument wrong, but it means I owe it more rigor, not less, and the reader should weigh it accordingly.

It's also worth questioning just how vast the economic power gap between expats and locals really is. Some fit my profile.

But many expats who come to Barcelona work to integrate their professional lives locally, earning local wages and dealing with the same questions of economic viability that their Spanish neighbors face. The gap between a Spanish software developer and a French one working at the same Barcelona startup is not the gap between a hedge fund manager and a factory worker. The "rich foreigner" framing flattens a diverse population into a single, convenient caricature.

There's an even more telling wrinkle: some of the loudest anti-guiri voices are guiris themselves. Expats who arrived years ago now complain about newer arrivals driving up rents and changing the character of their adopted neighborhoods. This is the "pulling up the ladder" phenomenon, and it has an exact American parallel, where immigrants who gained legal status advocate for stricter restrictions on those who came after them. In both cases, people who benefited from freedom of movement turn around and argue that others shouldn't have the same access. The scapegoating logic is so powerful it captures even the scapegoated group.

The implicit reasoning behind "expats just face friction, not oppression" is really just: *they have somewhere else to go and enough money to get there.* But that's the same argument restrictionists make about immigrants. *You chose to come here; you can choose to leave if you don't like the terms.* And it's worth noting that locals also have an exit option. The practical costs are asymmetric: uprooting from a city where your family, language, professional network, and community ties are rooted is harder than an expat returning to a country where those ties already exist. But the asymmetry of exit costs doesn't justify restricting the economic participation of one group based on where they were born. Both groups choose to stay. Neither is literally trapped.

Progressive politics rejects this argument when it's aimed downward on the economic ladder, recognizing that it treats freedom of movement as a privilege rather than a right. But it accepts the identical argument when aimed upward, because it *feels* different to restrict someone richer than you versus someone poorer.

The feeling is understandable. It's not an ethical argument.

## Culture, severity, and the ratchet

There's a version of this argument that isn't about economics at all: *the foreigners are displacing our culture.* Catalan and Spanish are being crowded out by English in commercial districts. Neighborhood bars become brunch spots. The texture of the place changes.

I take this seriously as a description. But what's being described is multiculturalism. Cultures have always mixed fluidly. The results are never "even" in the sense that every tradition survives in equal, static proportions. Some forms recede. New ones emerge. It's fair to acknowledge that market-driven cultural change can feel coercive to people with less purchasing power. When a neighborhood shifts to serve wealthier newcomers' preferences, English menus replacing Catalan ones, coworking spaces replacing neighborhood bars, locals who can't afford the new price level are effectively priced out of their own cultural landscape. That's a real loss, and dismissing it as progress would be glib. But the answer is to address the underlying market dynamics (housing supply, commercial rent policy, public space protections) rather than restricting who can show up. The desire to preserve cultural "purity" through origin-based restrictions is the same impulse whether it comes from a Catalan nationalist worried about English signage or an American nativist worried about Spanish being spoken in a Texas grocery store.

Does severity matter? Of course. Being deported is worse than facing a rent cap. A property purchase ban is not the same as detention. But the underlying logic is the same, and history shows that the severity tends to escalate once the logic is accepted. The escalation did not happen in a straight line. Trump's first term (2017-2021) began with rhetoric about "bad hombres," moved to a travel ban, and escalated to family separations at the border. The Biden administration reversed some of these policies, but the Overton window had shifted. Trump's second term picked up where the first left off, with mass deportation operations and attempts to revoke birthright citizenship. The pattern is not continuous escalation but a ratchet that resets higher after each cycle. Barcelona's policy arc, from protest slogans to rental bans to proposed foreign purchase restrictions, follows the same ratchet at a lower intensity. Accepting the principle that origin-based restrictions are legitimate, even mild ones, creates the precedent for harsher ones later.

## A darker precedent

The idea that scapegoating wealthier outsiders is somehow safer or more justified than scapegoating poorer ones has a grim historical track record. The most catastrophic example is antisemitism, which for centuries operated and continues to operate precisely on the logic that targeting an economically successful minority was "punching up" — that Jewish commercial success was itself the problem, that it came at the expense of the local population, and that restricting Jewish economic participation was a form of self-defense for the native majority.

The Rwandan genocide followed a strikingly similar structure. The Tutsi minority had historically occupied the higher economic and social strata, a hierarchy Belgian colonists hardened into rigid ethnic categories through mandatory identity cards. When the Hutu majority gained political power, the Tutsi were framed simultaneously as wealthy oppressors and as foreigners — not truly indigenous to Rwanda. Hutu Power propaganda designated them "oppressors of the Hutu" because of their perceived economic dominance, and cast their removal as liberation. The result was the murder of over 800,000 people in a hundred days.

I'm not predicting that anti-guiri sentiment in Barcelona will escalate to anything resembling antisemitism or the Rwandan genocide. The point is philosophical, not predictive. "Punching up" is often treated as inherently defensible. These are the starkest illustrations of why it isn't. The structural logic, *this group's relative wealth is the cause of our hardship, and restricting their economic participation is the solution*, is the same logic. When someone says "it's different because they're rich," they're making the exact argument that has historically justified targeting economically successful minorities. The direction of the economic gradient does not determine whether the move is just. Wealth-based discrimination is still discrimination. What determines whether a restriction is justified is not whether it targets people richer or poorer than you, but whether you're attributing systemic problems to a group defined by identity rather than addressing the systems themselves.

## This isn't a denial of power structures

The predictable objection to everything above is: *you're just a privileged person denying that power structures exist.* So let me be clear about where this argument comes from.

I've spent a long time thinking about power, starting with [an honors thesis on Nietzsche's theory of morality](/honors-thesis), which is essentially an extended study of how power operates through subtle forms of morality itself. Nietzsche's central insight in [*On the Genealogy of Morals*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_the_Genealogy_of_Morality) is that power isn't exercised only by the dominant. The priestly class, physically defeated by the aristocratic class, achieves a "radical revaluation of their enemies' values," redefining strength as evil and weakness as virtue. Power flows in both directions: the strong exert it through force, the weak exert it through moral frameworks that recast submission as righteousness and dominance as sin.

This matters here because both movements are, in Nietzschean terms, forms of [ressentiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resentment#Ressentiment) that have successfully moralized themselves. The anti-guiri claim isn't "we want to restrict foreigners' economic participation because it benefits us politically." It's "restricting foreigners is *justice*, it's punching up, it's decolonial, it's protecting the vulnerable." The MAGA version runs the same play in a different register: a movement of people who believe themselves disempowered by elites and outsiders reframes "they're taking our jobs" as "we're defending American workers," and immigration restriction becomes patriotism rather than exclusion. In both cases, the moral framing is the power move. And it's effective precisely because it's invisible as such.

The definition of who is disempowered rarely maps cleanly onto identity categories. A Barcelona local priced out of their neighborhood is disempowered. So is an expat subject to laws they have no political voice to shape. So is an undocumented worker in the US with no recourse against wage theft. The question isn't *which group deserves empowerment*, it's whether your framework for empowerment requires disempowering someone else defined by where they were born.

Recognizing that power structures are real, complex, and multidirectional is precisely what leads me to reject the binary framing that either the locals or the foreigners must win. Both can be empowered by better institutions and technologies. Neither needs to be scapegoated for the other to thrive.

## The false choice

There's a move that both left and right populism make here that needs to be named: framing this as a competition between the interests of locals and outsiders, as if addressing one group's concerns requires sacrificing the other's.

This is a false choice.

It's worth acknowledging where the anger is correctly aimed, even if it's misdirected at the wrong target. Spain's [golden visa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigrant_investor_programs) program, which from 2013 to 2025 granted residency to non-EU nationals who invested €500,000 or more in real estate, concentrated thousands of investment purchases in Barcelona, Madrid, and Málaga.[^23] The policy itself wasn't wrong: attracting capital and talent through open investment rules is exactly the kind of institution-level approach this essay advocates. The failure was pairing it with the same restrictive zoning, inadequate construction, and regulatory bottlenecks that constrain housing supply for everyone. Had Spain simultaneously deregulated its real estate development pipeline to let supply respond, golden visa investment would have expanded the housing stock rather than competing for a fixed one. Blaming the investors who responded to the incentive, rather than the governments that created the bottleneck, is a textbook case of scapegoating.

Some housing economists argue that non-resident speculation is a distinct market failure warranting targeted policy, as Canada and New Zealand have done. But the test for whether a policy is a market correction or a form of scapegoating is simple: does it target the behavior or the person's origin? A vacancy tax hits anyone who leaves a property empty, local or foreign. A ban on non-EU purchases hits only people born in the wrong place. The first is regulation. The second is discrimination with an economics paper attached.

This test applies to the economic participation of people already living in a community. Entry policy is a different question: visa regimes and immigration quotas inherently use origin as a criterion, and reasonable people can disagree about the right levels.[^25] The core claim holds for the populations both movements primarily target: people already participating in the local economy.

Housing scarcity and labor exploitation are not caused by the wrong people showing up. They are caused by rules that prevent supply from responding to demand: [zoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoning_in_the_United_States) that caps density, permitting regimes that take years, [rent controls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rent_regulation) that shrink the long-term rental stock, [occupational licensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_licensing) that locks workers out of legal employment. Remove the barriers and let people build, work, and trade freely. My own preference is for these deregulatory fixes. But even if you favor more interventionist solutions (public housing construction, inclusionary zoning mandates, rent stabilization), those are still better than scapegoating because they target the problem, not a group of people defined by where they were born.

The obvious objection is timing: structural reform takes years, and people's rents are rising now. That's fair. But the short-term toolkit doesn't require targeting anyone by nationality either. Vacancy taxes, short-term rental caps, emergency permitting fast-tracks for affordable housing, and enforcement against illegal tourist apartments all address the immediate pressure without sorting people by passport. The urgency of the crisis is real. It does not justify origin-based restrictions when behavior-based tools are available today. The classical liberal insight is that you design institutions around principles rather than around groups, precisely because the moment you start allocating rights and restrictions by group membership, you've created a hierarchy. Even if you believe you're creating it in the right direction.

The anti-guiri activist who wants to restrict foreign property ownership and the MAGA [restrictionist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immigration_reduction_in_the_United_States) who wants to limit immigration are making the same foundational move: defining a class of people by origin, then using collective political power to curtail that class's access to economic participation. The policy specifics differ. The underlying logic, *people from here deserve preferential access to economic life here*, is the same.

## What scapegoating actually is

The deepest parallel between these movements isn't ideological. It's functional.

Removing the zoning caps, permit bottlenecks, and regulatory barriers that prevent housing from being built is bureaucratically tedious and threatens entrenched interests. Opening legal labor markets means confronting the licensing regimes and work-authorization rules that keep supply artificially scarce. These fixes are slow, boring, and offer no emotional payoff. Blaming guiris or immigrants, by contrast, is viscerally satisfying and electorally useful. It gives voters a face to attach to their frustration and politicians a story simpler than "our institutional failures span decades and implicate all of us."

The scapegoating isn't a means to a policy end. In both cases, it *is* the product. It's what's being sold to voters. And the cost is borne by people who have no political voice to object, which, in a democracy that claims to care about rights, should be precisely the people we're most careful about targeting.

Either people have a legitimate claim to participate in the communities they live in regardless of national origin, or they don't. You can't make that principle conditional on wealth.

If you believe, as I do, that most immigration restrictions are themselves unjust, then "undocumented" is a legal status imposed by bad rules, not a moral failing. If you don't, the undocumented case requires a different argument than the one this essay makes.
