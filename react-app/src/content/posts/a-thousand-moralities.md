*The Revaluation — Part IV of VI*

The most serious challenge to Nietzsche's account of morality is also the most obvious one, and it has nothing to do with the internal consistency of his arguments. It's geographic.

The *Genealogy of Morals* tells a very specific story about a very specific historical sequence: a knightly-aristocratic caste whose values were inverted by a priestly caste through the mechanism of *ressentiment*, with the collision between Roman imperial power and Jewish monotheistic religion as the decisive pivot. "Rome against Judea, Judea against Rome." The entire narrative depends on particular features of the ancient Mediterranean world. Nietzsche grounds it in etymological evidence drawn from European languages and textual evidence drawn from European history. It is, unmistakably, a story about how the West became what it is.

The question is whether this is a universal account of how moralities form or a provincial one that Nietzsche mistook for universal. And the answer matters enormously, because if the master-slave dynamic is the template for all moral development, then Nietzsche has told us something fundamental about human nature. But if it's the template for one civilization's moral development only, then his philosophy — for all its brilliance — is a regional study dressed in universal rhetoric.

· · ·

Start with the most obvious counterexample. Confucian moral thought emerged from conditions that bear almost no resemblance to Nietzsche's schema. There is no slave revolt. There is no priestly caste waging spiritual revenge against a warrior aristocracy. Confucianism was articulated by members of the educated ruling class — Confucius himself was a minor nobleman and government official. The moral system it produced emphasizes hierarchy, filial piety, ritual propriety, and the cultivation of virtue through disciplined practice. It valorizes benevolence, but not in a way that resembles Christian compassion born of *ressentiment*. Confucian benevolence flows downward from the cultivated superior, and it presupposes rather than undermines social hierarchy.

On Nietzsche's framework, a morality articulated by the ruling class that affirms hierarchy and demands self-cultivation should look like a master morality. In some respects it does — the Confucian gentleman is defined by positive qualities he cultivates in himself, not by reactive negation of an enemy. But Confucianism also stresses restraint, duty, subordination of personal desire to social obligation, and care for others — qualities Nietzsche associates with life denial and the suppression of vital instincts. It doesn't fit neatly into either category. The master-slave dichotomy, which works tolerably well for the Roman-Christian transition, simply doesn't map onto the Chinese case.

Hindu civilization presents a different kind of challenge. The caste system does involve a priestly class — the Brahmins — at the top of the social hierarchy, and a warrior class — the Kshatriyas — below them. This looks superficially like Nietzsche's schema with the priests already victorious. But the Brahminical value system doesn't look like slave morality. It doesn't valorize weakness or suffering. It doesn't universalize compassion as the supreme virtue. It explicitly affirms hierarchy, duty according to caste, and the idea that different types of people have fundamentally different moral obligations. The warrior's dharma includes fighting and killing. The priest's dharma includes ritual purity and learning. There is no transvaluation in which priestly values delegitimize warrior values — both are affirmed within a single cosmological framework.

Japanese bushido combines elements that Nietzsche distributes across his two morality types in ways that shouldn't be possible on his account. It valorizes martial courage, honor, loyalty, and the willingness to die — clearly noble qualities. But it also demands extreme self-discipline, subordination to one's lord, and ritual suicide as atonement for failure — something that looks very close to the asceticism Nietzsche associates with priestly life denial. The samurai is simultaneously the blond beast and the ascetic priest, which Nietzsche's dichotomy doesn't allow for.

Islam emerged from a tribal warrior culture, was founded by a man who was both prophet and military leader, and produced a moral system that affirms conquest, courage, justice, and submission to God simultaneously. Muhammad is neither a Roman aristocrat nor a Jewish priest in Nietzsche's sense. He is something the framework has no category for.

> If Christian morality were the natural expression of something fundamental about human moral reality, then every civilization that achieved complexity and duration would converge on something resembling it. They don't. Not even approximately.

Indigenous and oral-tradition moral systems pose perhaps the deepest challenge of all. Many don't have sharp caste divisions between priests and warriors. Many don't have a concept of individual moral guilt in the Christian sense. Many organize moral life around kinship obligations, reciprocity, and relationship to the land — categories that don't map onto any of Nietzsche's distinctions. The moral universe of Aboriginal Australian songline culture or Amazonian perspectivism operates on premises so different from the European tradition that applying the master-slave framework isn't just inaccurate. It's a category error.

· · ·

So is Nietzsche's philosophy fatally provincial? The answer, I think, is no — but only if you're willing to revise what the philosophy claims, separating the universal engine from the particular narrative.

The strongest version of Nietzsche's position is this: the will to power always drives the formation of morality, but it does so in radically different ways depending on historical circumstances. The will to power is formally universal — all living things seek to discharge strength, overcome resistance, extend themselves, impose form on chaos — but materially particular. It doesn't produce the same moral configurations everywhere because it always operates within specific conditions. The will to power flowing through a desert tribal culture with a charismatic prophetic leader produces Islam. Flowing through a settled agrarian civilization with a literate aristocratic class produces Confucianism. Flowing through the collision of Roman power and Jewish monotheism produces Christianity and the slave revolt. The driving force is the same. The outcomes are radically different because the channels are different.

This is actually more consistent with what Nietzsche says about the will to power in other contexts. When he discusses art, he doesn't claim that the will to power always produces the same kind of art. Greek tragedy, Renaissance painting, Beethoven — these are all expressions of the will to power but they look nothing alike because they emerge from different conditions. There is no reason morality should be different.

And this version handles the counterexamples well. Confucian ethics doesn't need to be master morality or slave morality — it's the will to power expressing itself through a social formation in which the ruling class maintained power through ritual competence and moral authority rather than through priestly revenge or physical domination. The power dynamic is real — Confucian ethics served to legitimate and perpetuate elite rule — but the mechanism is distinctive. Bushido doesn't need to be an incoherent hybrid of noble and priestly elements. The samurai's asceticism isn't life denial — it's a technology for producing more effective warriors, will to power in service of ascending life even when it looks superficially like the self-mortification Nietzsche associates with priestly decadence. The form resembles Christian asceticism. The function is entirely different.

This revised Nietzsche is more modest in his specific claims but potentially more powerful in his application. He gives up the grand narrative of Rome against Judea as the universal template and gains a genuinely flexible analytical method that can be applied to any moral system in any culture. He preserves the will to power as the universal driving force while acknowledging that the forms it takes are as varied as life itself.

· · ·

But here's the turn that matters most, and the one I didn't see when I was writing my thesis twenty years ago.

The sheer diversity of the world's moral systems doesn't just challenge Nietzsche's specific narrative. It devastates the claim that Christian morality — or its secular descendants in human rights discourse and progressive egalitarianism — represents something universal about human moral reality.

China built and maintained one of the most sophisticated civilizations in human history for thousands of years on moral foundations that look nothing like Christian morality. Confucian ethics doesn't valorize the weak over the strong, doesn't universalize compassion as the supreme virtue, doesn't generate guilt through the fiction of free will, doesn't posit the equality of all souls before God. And it worked. It organized social life, sustained political institutions, produced art and philosophy and science of extraordinary depth. The same is true of Hindu civilization, of the Islamic world during its golden age, of pre-contact civilizations in the Americas.

If the moral framework that dominates the modern West — compassion, equality, universal rights, the moral priority of the suffering — were the natural expression of something inherent in human moral perception, then civilizations that achieved comparable sophistication would converge on something resembling it. The convergences that actually exist across cultures are far more minimal than what Christianity claims as morally essential: prohibitions on intra-group killing, norms of reciprocity, some form of fairness. The elaborate superstructure of sin, guilt, redemption, universal equality before God, and the moral priority of the meek and suffering is specific to one tradition and its derivatives. It is not a human universal.

This is the simplest and most decisive argument against the permanent authority of our inherited moral framework, and it requires no philosophical sophistication at all. It is an empirical observation. The person who wants to defend the universal validity of Western moral assumptions — whether in their Christian or secular progressive form — bears a burden of proof they rarely acknowledge. They need to explain why their moral configuration should be treated as uniquely correct when other configurations, grounded in entirely different premises, sustained civilizations of comparable depth and duration.

> The default assumption should be the Nietzschean one: this morality, like every other, is a human creation that emerged under specific historical conditions and serves specific functions, and its claim to universality is itself one of the tools by which it maintains its authority.

And this connects back to the will to power in a way that strengthens the argument considerably. If diverse moral systems are all expressions of the will to power shaped by different historical conditions, then the Christian claim to unique moral truth is itself an expression of will to power — specifically, the will to power of a moral system that discovered that claiming universality is an extraordinarily effective strategy for self-perpetuation. Telling people that your moral code isn't one option among many but the truth about how all human beings should live is a power move of the highest order. Through missionary activity, colonialism, and the secular universalization of Christian moral premises through human rights discourse, one provincial moral tradition has extended its reach across almost the entire globe. Nietzsche saw this. He observed that the morality of pity had become so thoroughly victorious that it "no longer sees itself." The comparative evidence makes visible what that victory obscured.

· · ·

This reframing also transforms the question of what comes after. Nietzsche's call for a revaluation of all values can sound purely philosophical — an abstract demand to question one's deepest moral commitments. But set against the comparative evidence, it becomes something more concrete. It's an invitation to look at the full range of moral possibilities that human civilizations have actually explored and ask whether the one we inherited is really the one best suited to our circumstances.

And the comparative perspective reveals that there are real resources available for this reevaluation, not just Nietzsche's somewhat sketchy gestures toward the blond beast and the sovereign individual. Confucian thought offers a sophisticated account of moral cultivation through hierarchy, ritual, and self-discipline that doesn't depend on guilt or the valorization of weakness. Stoic and Epicurean philosophy offer practices of self-transformation that are post-metaphysical without being nihilistic. Hindu and Buddhist thought offer accounts of self-overcoming that parallel Nietzsche's in striking ways but have been developed with far more practical detail over far longer periods. The warrior codes of various traditions — bushido, the martial honor traditions of the ancient world — offer accounts of excellence, discipline, and self-mastery that provide more concrete content than Nietzsche's noble type ever received.

The irony is considerable — and personal. When I wrote my thesis at Bowdoin, I concluded that Nietzsche's revaluation failed partly because nobody had articulated the affirmative vision he gestured toward. I surveyed the philosophers in Nietzsche's own lineage — Heidegger, Bataille, Deleuze, Foucault — and found that each developed the critical dimension with great sophistication but left the affirmative vision underdetermined. The [final essay](/posts/the-experiment) in this series will examine what each of them tried and why they fell short. But the deeper problem was that I, like them, was looking in the wrong place. The actual articulations of non-Christian, non-egalitarian, life-affirming moral visions already exist in abundance. They come from traditions that the European philosophical canon has largely ignored or treated as anthropological curiosities rather than as live options for moral life.

This doesn't mean any of these traditions can be imported wholesale. They emerged under their own historical conditions and carry their own limitations. But they demonstrate that the moral landscape is far richer than the choice between Christian morality and nihilism that Nietzsche sometimes presents as the only options. The revaluation he calls for doesn't require inventing something from nothing. It requires the honesty to recognize that what we have is one option among many, and the courage to ask whether it's still the best one.

Nietzsche spent enormous intellectual energy trying to demonstrate through philosophical argument and historical narrative that Christian morality is contingent rather than necessary. The existence of a thousand other moralities settles the question empirically — quietly, decisively, and in a way that no amount of philosophical counterargument can undo.
