Translation isn't adjudication.

AI can summarize the architecture for the PM, the design system for the engineer, and the positioning for the designer. It can keep three excellent specialists fluent in each other's work without a single coordination meeting. What it can't do is decide which one wins when the summary reveals that all three are right inside their own domain and incoherent across them.

A new feature lands. Positioning says it should be approachable for non-technical users. The design system says dense, information-rich patterns appropriate for power users. The architecture says the natural implementation is a configuration file, which is neither. Nobody made an error in their own domain. The tension lives between the domains.

In the old org, this got worked out in a meeting. Someone arbitrated, or the group iterated until a resolution emerged. Slow, but the answer lived in the heads of the people in the room rather than in any durable artifact. In the async parallel structure that AI now enables, the same tension can compound invisibly for weeks before anyone calls it a product problem.

The architecture requires a role most org charts don't name explicitly: a reconciler. Often a senior cross-functional lead, often the founder. Their job isn't to attend more meetings. It is to maintain the rubric – the explicit precedence order among competing disciplinary goods. Approachable beats dense for this product's user, in these contexts, because of these commitments. Specific enough to resolve the same trade-off the same way twice.

The rubric is not a values poster or a strategy deck. It is the written version of trade-offs leaders have historically kept fuzzy on purpose, and that fuzziness is the cultural blocker, not the technical one. Most companies won't do this even when they fully understand the architecture.

The good news: nobody authors a rubric in a vacuum. It accumulates through adjudication. A single trade-off resolved once is an expense. The same pattern recognized across three resolutions and codified is an investment that retires a class of future expenses. Constitution by case law.

The infrastructure underneath is write integrity – append-only history, provenance on every change, schema, cross-references between sections. Without it, six months of careful calibration gets quietly corrupted by two weeks of uncoordinated edits, and the rubric decays faster than the team can maintain it.
